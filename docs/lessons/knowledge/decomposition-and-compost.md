# KNOWLEDGE, SKILLS & CULTURE: Decomposition and Compost

## Activity Summary

When a leaf falls or an apple core is thrown away, it doesn't just vanish — an army of tiny living things eats it and turns it back into soil. This is **decomposition**, one of the most important processes on Earth: it recycles dead things into food for new life. In this lesson the child builds a see-through "rot jar" and watches decomposition happen over days and weeks, discovering that soil is *alive* and that nothing in nature is truly wasted. The lesson stays on one focused idea: dead stuff gets broken down by decomposers and becomes new soil.

## Vocabulary

**Decomposition**: The breaking-down of dead plants, food, and animals into simpler stuff that goes back into the soil. Rotting is decomposition — and it's a good thing, not a gross thing.

**Decomposer**: A living thing whose job is to eat dead material — earthworms, roly-polies, mushrooms and molds (fungi), and bacteria too small to see. Decomposers are nature's recycling crew.

**Fungi**: A whole kingdom of living things that includes mushrooms and mold. The fuzzy stuff that grows on old bread is fungi decomposing it. Fungi send out tiny threads that dissolve dead material and drink it up.

**Nutrients**: The good food-parts inside dead material. Decomposers release nutrients back into the soil, where roots drink them up so new plants can grow.

**Compost**: Decomposition you make on purpose. When you pile up food scraps and leaves and let decomposers work, it turns into dark, crumbly, nutrient-rich compost — you can hold last month's banana peel in your hand as new soil.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

The child builds a clear "rot jar" with a piece of fruit or a leaf plus a little soil, then observes it change over days and weeks. Alongside it they set up a *control* — the same food sealed away from soil, or a non-living object like a plastic cap — to discover that decomposers (from the soil) are what do the work. The activity *is* the concept: they watch decomposition happen.

**Activity Type**: Hands-on setup + long-term observation

**Duration**: 15 minutes to build, then 1 minute every few days for 2-4 weeks

**Setup time**: Minimal

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- 2 clear jars or cups with loose lids (air can get in — don't seal tight)
- A little garden soil (real outdoor soil, which contains decomposers)
- A piece of fruit or vegetable (apple slice, strawberry) and a leaf
- A non-living item for comparison (a small plastic bottle cap or bit of foil)

**Optional**:
- A magnifying glass
- A spray bottle to keep things slightly damp
- A real earthworm or two (returned to the garden after)

**Cost**: Free

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Scoop a little outdoor soil into one jar. Keep a second jar clean/empty.
2. Have the food, leaf, and plastic cap ready.

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 min):
   Hold up an apple slice. "When food gets thrown outside, where does it go? It doesn't disappear — tiny living things eat it and turn it into soil. That's called decomposition. Let's watch it happen." Smell a pinch of the outdoor soil: "This dark soil is full of decomposers — worms, mold, and bacteria too small to see."

2. **Build the rot jar** (5-6 min):
   Put the apple slice and leaf on the soil in the first jar. Dampen slightly and rest the lid on top loosely (air needs to get in). In the *second* jar, place the plastic cap (a non-living thing) on a bit of soil. "Now we have an experiment. Which one will get broken down — the apple, or the plastic? Let's guess, then find out."

3. **Predict and observe** (3-4 min):
   Ask the child to predict. Mark the jars with today's date. Set them somewhere warm where you'll see them daily. "We'll check every few days. Watch for mold — that fuzzy stuff is fungi, a kind of decomposer, doing its job."

4. **Wrap-up** (2-3 min):
   "Decomposers are nature's recycling crew. They take dead things apart and give the good parts — the nutrients — back to the soil, so new plants can grow. Nothing in nature gets wasted." Revisit the jars over the coming weeks and narrate the changes.

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
- Keep it to one word repeated: "rot" or "break down"
- The daily peek is the whole point — short and curious ("What's different today?")
- Skip the control jar if it's confusing; just watch the fruit change

**3-4+ years**:
- Emphasize the experiment: apple vs. plastic — only the living/once-living thing decomposes
- Introduce fungi and bacteria by name when mold appears
- Talk about the nutrient cycle: dead → decomposers → soil → new plants → repeat
- Draw the jar every few days to record the change

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1 — Start a real compost**: Keep a small lidded bucket for fruit and veggie scraps. Over weeks, watch it turn dark and crumbly. Use the finished compost on a plant (ties to the Seeds and germination lesson).

**Variation 2 — Decomposer hunt**: Lift a rock or log outside and look for roly-polies, worms, and centipedes — the decomposer crew at work. Return everything gently.

**Repeat play**: Try decomposing different things (bread, leaf, orange peel, a piece of paper) and compare which breaks down fastest.

## Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: The child is grossed out by mold.
**Solution**: Reframe it: mold is a living thing (fungi) doing an important job. Keep the lid on loosely so smell is contained, and observe through the glass rather than touching. Wash hands after.

**Challenge**: Nothing seems to change.
**Solution**: Decomposition is slow — give it 1-2 weeks and keep it slightly damp and warm. Soft fruit (strawberry, banana) breaks down faster than a hard apple. Real outdoor soil (with decomposers) works far better than store-bought sterile potting mix.

**Challenge**: Worried about pests or smell indoors.
**Solution**: Use small amounts, keep lids loosely on, and set the jars on a windowsill or garage shelf. Toss everything into the outdoor garden or trash when the observation is done.

### Follow-Up Activities
1. At mealtime, sort food scraps into "this will decompose" vs. "this won't" (banana peel vs. plastic wrapper).
2. On a walk in fall, dig into a pile of old leaves and find the dark, half-rotted layer underneath — decomposition in the wild.
3. Connect to Seeds and germination: compost feeds the soil, the soil feeds the seed — the circle of life, literally.
