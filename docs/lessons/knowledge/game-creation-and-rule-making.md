## üéØ Activity Summary

Children design and create their own simple games with rules they establish together. Whether it's a racing game with household items, a memory matching game, or a movement game, children exercise decision-making, creative thinking, and social negotiation skills. This activity develops executive function, creative problem-solving, and social-emotional learning while showing children that they have agency in creating their own experiences.

## Vocabulary

**Strategy**: A plan or way to do something to achieve a goal, like deciding how to move your toy car fastest in your game

**Negotiate**: To talk together and agree on something, like when you and a friend decide what the rules should be

**Design**: To create and plan something new, like when you invent your own game from scratch

**Rule**: A direction or guideline that tells you what you can and cannot do in a game or activity

## Activity

### Activity Overview

In this hands-on activity, children explore materials and design their own game from start to finish. They'll make decisions about what the game is, how to play it, and what the rules are. Then they'll test their game and adjust the rules as they go. This builds creative thinking, decision-making skills, and teaches children that they can create the experiences they enjoy.

**Activity Type**: Social activity, creative expression, interpersonal practice

**Duration**: 20-35 minutes (with natural breaks)

**Setup time**: 5 minutes (gather materials)

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- Common household objects (blocks, toy cars, stuffed animals, balls, cushions) - a variety of items
- Container or basket for game pieces
- Simple paper or cardstock (optional) for making a game board

**Optional**:
- Markers or crayons for decorating
- Dice or spinners (or make one from paper) for adding chance elements
- Timer or bell for timed rounds
- Tokens or counters (buttons, coins, cereal pieces)
- Small rewards (stickers, stamps, high-fives)

**Cost**: Free to under $5 (using household items)

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Gather a variety of household items that could become game pieces (blocks, toys, small objects) and place them in a basket or container
2. Have basic art supplies nearby (paper, markers, crayons)
3. Identify and clear a safe play space where children can move around freely

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 minutes):
   Start with excitement: "Today WE get to be game designers! We're going to make our very own game!" Show the basket of materials and ask open-ended questions: "What kinds of games could we make with these? What's your favorite game? What makes it fun?" Explain that they get to decide the rules and what makes their game fun.

2. **Exploration & Idea Generation** (5-7 minutes):
   Open the basket and let the child handle and examine items. Ask: "What do you notice about these things? How could they move? Stack? Hide?" Listen to ideas and build on them: "If blocks are mountains, what would we need to do in our game?" Model creative thinking: "I'm wondering... could we stack these to make an obstacle?"

3. **Game Design & Rule-Making** (7-10 minutes):
   Help the child narrow down to one game idea. Co-create the rules by asking key questions: Where does the game start? Where does it end? How do we move? What happens if something goes wrong? How do we know who wins? Document the rules simply with drawings or words. Encourage creative thinking: "Should we have a silly rule that makes everyone laugh?"

4. **Play Testing & Refinement** (5-8 minutes):
   Play the game together. Observe and comment on what you notice. After one round, pause and ask: "What was fun? What was tricky? Do we want to change any rules?" Be flexible with rule adjustments. Play at least one more round with any new rules. Your role is to be an enthusiastic participant and co-creator, playing by their rules exactly as stated.

5. **Wrap-Up** (3-5 minutes):
   Celebrate the accomplishment: "We made a game! You designed this game and made the rules!" Ask a reflection question: "What was your favorite part? What made it fun?" Offer to play again or create a new game next time. Make cleanup part of the fun by turning it into a game itself.

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
Keep the game extremely simple with just one action (push, throw, stack, hide/find). Provide more structure by offering 2-3 material choices instead of a full basket. Make rules very concrete and tied to physical space: "We push this car to the couch." Expect rules to focus on what you DO rather than winning/losing. Use lots of repetition and high adult involvement - you're a co-player who helps guide and demonstrate.

**3-4+ years**:
Introduce more complex rule types like turn-taking, scoring, and obstacles. Encourage thinking about winning conditions: "How does someone win this game?" Support creation of a game board with a simple drawn path. Introduce consequences or special spaces: "If you land here, you have to hop like a bunny." Encourage them to write or draw their rules. Play with multiple children to practice rule negotiation. Ask them to teach the game to someone else to reinforce their thinking.

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1: Sensory Game**
Create a game based on sensory experiences: "What if we played with our eyes closed? Can we find things by touching?" Or organize materials by texture and create a "feely game."

**Variation 2: Movement Game**
Design a game focused on movement with jumping, spinning, crawling, or balancing. Create trick spaces where you must perform a silly movement to continue.

**Variation 3: Story Game**
Create a game where movement tells a story: "We're dinosaurs walking through the jungle, and we have to avoid getting stuck in the mud!" Combine imagination with game mechanics.

**Variation 4: Outdoor Game Adventure**
Take game creation outside using natural materials like sticks, rocks, leaves, and grass. Create games using the landscape (jump from stone to stone, hide-and-seek, nature scavenger hunt with rules).

**Repeat play**: Some children love creating a NEW game every time you do this activity. Others develop favorite games they want to play repeatedly. Both approaches are wonderful and show creative thinking developing in different ways.

## üìù Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child wants you to make the rules rather than creating them themselves.
**Solution**: Gently redirect with questions: "Hmm, what do YOU think?" and "I want to know YOUR idea!" Start with small decisions (pick one of two options) to build confidence. Use phrases like "You're the game designer - you decide!"

**Challenge**: The game doesn't have clear rules or seems to lack structure.
**Solution**: This is completely developmentally normal! Young children may focus on the joy of play rather than formal rules. The value is in the creative process, not in having a "perfect" game. Celebrate what they created.

**Challenge**: Frequent rule changes mid-game; child keeps modifying rules.
**Solution**: Embrace this! "You're making the game even better!" If you want to establish consistency, try: "Let's play with these rules for one whole round, and THEN we can change them if we want."

**Challenge**: Conflict over rules when multiple children are playing.
**Solution**: Facilitate negotiations: "You both have good ideas. Can we use both rules? What if we try it YOUR way first, then HIS way?" This teaches democratic decision-making and compromise.

### Follow-Up Activities

1. **Play again tomorrow**: Ask if they want to play the same game again or create a new one. Both reinforce learning in different ways.

2. **Document their creation**: Take a photo of the game setup or written rules. Create a special "Game Creator" album or display the rules on the refrigerator.

3. **Share with family**: Let the child teach the game to a grandparent or sibling. This reinforces their learning and shows you value their creation.
