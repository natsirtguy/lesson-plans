## üéØ Activity Summary

Through listening, speaking, and interactive play, children develop foundational communication skills while building vocabulary and confidence. Using songs, storytelling, games, and conversation, children practice pronunciation, expand their language repertoire, and establish the critical foundation for literacy development.

## Vocabulary

**Articulation**: The way sounds are made and produced with your mouth, lips, and tongue. When you say the word "cat," you're articulating each sound clearly.

**Dialogue**: A back-and-forth conversation between two people where one person speaks, then the other responds. When you ask your child "What's this?" and they answer, you're having a dialogue.

**Phonological Awareness**: The ability to recognize, identify, and play with the individual sounds in words. When you notice that "cat" and "bat" both start with the same sound, that's phonological awareness.

**Expression**: Conveying ideas, feelings, or messages through language, gestures, and tone of voice. When a child says "I happy!" with a big smile, they're showing expression.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

Children develop communication skills through listening, speaking, and word recognition in playful, interactive contexts. They practice naming objects, hearing words repeated in meaningful contexts, and building confidence in their own attempts to speak. This foundational language work directly supports school readiness, social connection, and cognitive development.

**Activity Type**: Communication/Interactive learning/Guided discovery

**Duration**: 15-25 minutes (with natural breaks)

**Setup time**: 5-10 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- Picture cards or real objects (10-15 items) - simple, clear images or actual objects from around the house
- Children's picture books - colorful with minimal text and engaging illustrations
- Musical instrument or music player - bells, shakers, or smartphone/tablet with songs

**Optional**:
- Puppet or stuffed animal - engages imagination and encourages dialogue
- Flannel board with felt pieces - makes stories interactive and tactile
- Mirror - helps children see mouth movements for pronunciation modeling
- Scarves or ribbons - adds movement and sensory engagement to language practice

**Cost**: Free to under $10 (most items already at home)

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Choose 5-8 target words appropriate to your child's current level (familiar objects like ball, dog, eat, mama, or foods they eat)
2. Gather picture cards, real objects, or prepare props
3. Select a simple song or rhyme you know well
4. Ensure comfortable, distraction-free seating or floor space
5. Arrange seating in a circle or facing position for face-to-face interaction
6. Position yourself at child's eye level to encourage eye contact
7. Ensure good lighting so child can see your mouth movements

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 min):
   - Start with a familiar song or greeting ("Hello, hello! How are you?")
   - Sing or speak with animated facial expressions to capture attention
   - Point to yourself and say your name, then point to child and say their name
   - Make eye contact and smile to create safe, welcoming interaction
   - Explain simply: "We're going to learn new words and have fun talking together!"

2. **Exploration** (12-18 min):

   **Phase 1: Listening and Recognition** (4-6 minutes)
   - Show picture cards or real objects one at a time
   - Say the word clearly and naturally, point to it, repeat 2-3 times
   - Wait for child to respond verbally, with a gesture, or by pointing
   - Use exaggerated facial expressions and slow speech for clarity
   - Celebrate any attempt: "Yes! Cat! You said cat!"

   **Phase 2: Guided Practice** (4-6 minutes)
   - Ask simple questions: "Can you point to the cat?" or "What is this?"
   - If child doesn't respond, model the answer and try again
   - Use a puppet or stuffed animal to ask questions in a playful voice
   - Sing the words to a familiar tune to make practice memorable
   - Use gestures and actions to demonstrate meaning (cat = meow and scratch motions)
   - Accept approximations - "ca" for "cat" shows learning and effort

   **Phase 3: Free Expression and Play** (4-6 minutes)
   - Let child choose which picture or object to name
   - Encourage child to make animal sounds or actions associated with words
   - Create simple "conversations" with back-and-forth exchange
   - Use real objects for child to hold and explore while naming them
   - Step back and listen more than you talk - give space for child's attempts
   - When child gets stuck, provide the word without judgment

3. **Discovery** (2-3 min):
   - Review favorite words together: "What was your favorite word we learned?"
   - Notice which words captured their interest most
   - Make connections: "We saw the cat, and it went meow!"
   - Point out how words are used in daily life: "You heard me say 'cat' - look, here's a cat!"

4. **Wrap-Up** (2-3 min):
   - Use a closing song or phrase: "We learned so many words today!"
   - Sing or chant the new words one final time together
   - Celebrate effort: "You did such a great job practicing today!"
   - Incorporate cleanup into language: "Let's put the cards away. Can you put the cat away?" (continues language reinforcement)

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
- Focus on 3-5 high-frequency words (familiar objects like ball, dog, eat, mama)
- Use very simple one-word labels rather than sentences
- Incorporate actions with every word to make language multisensory
- Accept and celebrate any vocalization or sound attempt
- Keep sessions shorter (10-15 minutes) with frequent breaks
- Provide very high adult support with lots of modeling, repetition, and encouragement

**3-4+ years**:
- Introduce 8-12 words per session
- Practice two-word combinations: "big dog," "eat apple," "mommy's shoe"
- Ask more complex questions: "What color is the cat?" or "Where is the ball?"
- Introduce simple rhyming or word families (cat, bat, hat)
- Encourage child to tell simple stories about pictures
- Practice turn-taking conversations with more back-and-forth exchange
- Play simple word games like "I Spy" or "What's Missing?"
- Children often engage 20-25 minutes at this age

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1: Picture Walk and Talk**
Take a walk around the house or outside, stopping to name things you see together. Point out and name items: "Look! A tree! What color is the tree?" This brings language learning into natural environments and reinforces words in real contexts.

**Variation 2: Sound Effects and Onomatopoeia**
Focus on the sounds animals and objects make (meow, moo, splash, pop). Children love making silly sounds, which develops phonological awareness essential for reading readiness.

**Variation 3: Category Sorting**
Once child knows several words, practice grouping them: animals, foods, clothes, things in bathroom. Ask "Is this an animal or food?" to deepen understanding of word relationships.

**Thematic variations**:
- Animals - practice animal names and sounds throughout the week
- Food/Mealtime - learn food words during snack or meal prep
- Body Parts - teach during bath time while naturally touching body parts
- Clothes and Dressing - practice clothing words while getting dressed
- Household Routines - label activities during daily routines (washing hands, brushing teeth)
- Seasonal - learn weather words and seasonal objects in real context
- Feelings and Emotions - use face cards or puppet to practice emotion words

## üìù Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child doesn't attempt to repeat words; seems to only listen

**Solution**: This is developmentally normal and actually important. Children learn language through listening long before speaking. Comprehension develops before production. Continue modeling and celebrating any response (pointing, nodding, eye contact). Don't pressure speaking. Keep repeating naturally without expecting immediate repetition. A child who understands but doesn't yet speak is learning.

**Challenge**: Child gets frustrated or loses interest quickly

**Solution**: Reduce number of words (try 3 instead of 8). Follow the child's interests - if they're fascinated by the dog picture, spend more time with that. Take a break and try again later. Ensure child is rested and fed before language-intensive activities. Keep it playful and fun, not rigid.

**Challenge**: Child mispronounces words or has speech differences

**Solution**: Speech development is gradual - all toddlers and preschoolers have immature speech. Model correct pronunciation naturally without correcting the child ("You said 'ca' - yes, that's a cat!"). Don't repeat their mispronunciation back to them. If you have concerns about speech development, discuss with your pediatrician while continuing normal language play without pressure.

**Challenge**: Sibling or other children interrupt or dominate the activity

**Solution**: Include siblings at appropriate level if possible. Let them "help" teach the target child. Or save one-on-one language time for nap time or evening. Even small pockets of focused time are valuable.

### Follow-Up Activities

1. **Daily narration**: Throughout the day, name objects and actions as you do them ("Mommy's putting on shoes," "We're eating apple"). Natural repetition in real contexts helps words stick better than isolated practice.

2. **Multilingual family connection**: If your family speaks multiple languages, mix languages naturally during play. Share songs, nursery rhymes, and stories from your cultural background. Teach words from grandparents' native language when possible. Multiple language exposure creates cognitive advantages and honors family heritage.

3. **Language journal**: Keep a simple record noting new words child attempts or masters. Jot down a few lines: "Today Maya said 'dog' and 'cat' unprompted!" Share progress with family members and other caregivers to keep language learning consistent across environments.
