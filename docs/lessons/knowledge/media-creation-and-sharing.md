## üéØ Activity Summary

Media creation and sharing invites young children to produce content (photos, videos, recordings, digital art) and intentionally share it with others. Children will experience being creators and communicators, learning that they have something meaningful to express and that others enjoy seeing their creations.

## Vocabulary

**Creator**: Someone who makes or produces something new, like a photo, video, drawing, or song. When you take a picture with a camera, you are being a creator.

**Audience**: The people who see, watch, or listen to something you made. Your family members might be your audience when you share a video with them.

**Media**: Different ways to share messages and stories with others, such as photos, videos, recordings, or drawings. A photo is one type of media.

**Share**: To give or show something to someone else so they can enjoy it too. When you show a photo to Grandma, you are sharing it with her.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

Children will create media (photos, videos, or audio recordings) using a smartphone or tablet, then intentionally share their creation with trusted family members. This builds confidence, digital literacy, and understanding of audience while keeping child agency and safety at the center.

**Activity Type**: Digital Creation/Communication/Creative Expression

**Duration**: 15-30 minutes (varies based on creation type)

**Setup time**: 5-10 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- Smartphone or tablet with camera
- Age-appropriate camera app, video app, or drawing app
- Subjects to photograph or film (child, toys, family members, outdoor scenery)
- Pre-approved list of people to share with (trusted family, close friends)

**Optional**:
- Ring light or natural window light for better quality
- Simple props (hats, scarves, stuffed animals)
- Filters or stickers in camera app
- Simple video editing app (iMovie, CapCut - with adult support)
- Microphone for clearer audio recording

**Cost**: Free-$5

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Choose what type of creation (photo, video, drawing, or audio recording)
2. Test all apps and ensure they work properly
3. Create a pre-approved list of 3-5 trusted people who can receive the child's creations
4. Prepare subjects and props if needed
5. Ensure good lighting for photos or videos
6. Safety check: Enable device restrictions to limit app access

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 min):
   Build excitement about being a creator: "Today you're a filmmaker/photographer/artist!" Show examples of simple media your child has created before or enjoys. Explain who will see their creation and why. Set clear expectations: "We'll make one photo/video, then we're done. There's no perfect way‚Äîyou get to decide what to make."

2. **Creation** (8-15 min):
   Help your child brainstorm what to create ("What would you like to show people?"). Guide the technical use of the device without directing the content. For photos: help frame the shot and ensure good lighting. For videos: keep the recording short (30-60 seconds) and the camera steady. For audio: help record singing, talking, or sounds. Celebrate their choices as they make them: "I love how you arranged those toys!" Allow them to review and retake if they want.

3. **Selection** (2-5 min):
   If multiple shots were taken, review together and ask which one they like best and why. Apply simple edits if interested (stickers, filters, frames). Add a caption or title with their approval. Finalize with their approval.

4. **Wrap-up** (2-3 min):
   Celebrate the creation and review what they made. Discuss who will see it and why. Show how it will be sent. Consider printing a copy or saving it in a special folder. Plan follow-up: "Maybe they'll send you a message about it later."

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
Focus on very simple, single-action creation (one photo, one short video, one song). Adult holds the device and helps the child aim/frame. Keep recordings very short (15-30 seconds maximum). Minimal editing; share immediately after creating. Choose familiar subjects (their toy, parent's face, family pet). Remember that the process is the point; the child may not care much about the final product. Keep device use brief to prevent overstimulation.

**3-4+ years**:
Child can help hold the device with support and help aim/frame. Can create longer recordings (30-60 seconds or more). Can use simple editing features (filters, stickers, basic transitions). Interested in planning what to create before starting. Understands who will see it and why. May want to make multiple creations in one session. Can help with simple editing decisions and review their work.

## üìù Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child is camera-shy or refuses to be filmed/photographed.
**Solution**: Don't force it. Start by filming their toys or favorite objects instead. Film from behind so only their back shows. Create video content about things they love rather than about them. Normalize the camera by having it out in the background without pressure. Consider audio recording (singing, talking) instead of video.

**Challenge**: Child wants to film inappropriate content or keep going forever.
**Solution**: Set clear limits before starting: "We'll make three photos, then we're done." Use a visual timer. Establish family rules about what's appropriate to share. Discuss privacy: "That's something just for us, not for sharing."

**Challenge**: Recipient doesn't respond to shared media, and child is disappointed.
**Solution**: Choose recipients known to respond. Check in with them beforehand. If someone doesn't respond, explain kindly without blame. Follow up manually if needed: "Aunt Susan loved your video!"

**Challenge**: Child is more interested in the device than the creative content.
**Solution**: Limit device time overall. Make the creation goal clear and concrete. Redirect focus to the content/subject rather than the screen. Take breaks if the child gets too focused on the device itself.

### Follow-Up Activities
1. Create a digital album or folder to organize and review their creations together
2. Print favorite creations and display in a family photo gallery
3. Send periodic "check-in" videos or photos to distant family members to stay connected
