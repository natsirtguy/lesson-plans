# KNOWLEDGE, SKILLS & CULTURE: Party Planning and Hosting

## Activity Summary

Children engage in the planning, preparation, and hosting of a pretend or real celebration event, creating invitations, decorations, and a welcoming space for guests. This activity develops organizational thinking, decision-making skills, fine motor abilities, and social-emotional competence while teaching the concept of hospitality and the joy of creating special moments for people they care about.

## Vocabulary

**Hospitality**: The kind and friendly way you welcome and take care of guests to make them feel special and loved. When you offer someone a snack or show them around your party space, you are showing hospitality.

**Orchestrate**: To plan and organize all the different parts of something so they work together beautifully. When you decide what decorations to put where and when to serve snacks, you are orchestrating your party.

**Coordinate**: Making sure different things work together smoothly and at the right time. When you plan decorations that match your party theme, you are coordinating your ideas.

**Welcoming**: Creating a warm, friendly, and happy environment where people feel safe, comfortable, and wanted. A welcoming space has decorations, friendly greetings, and snacks ready to enjoy.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

In this hands-on dramatic play activity, children plan and host a celebration event with invitations, decorations, refreshments, and guests. They learn to make decisions about party themes, create decorations and invitations, arrange a welcoming space, and practice hospitality skills by greeting and caring for their guests.

**Activity Type**: Dramatic play / Social activity / Cultural practice

**Duration**: 20-45 minutes (depending on age and interest)

**Setup time**: 15-20 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- Paper (construction paper, cardstock) in various colors
- Markers, crayons, or colored pencils
- Child-safe scissors
- Glue stick or tape
- Decorations (streamers, balloons, stickers, tissue paper, ribbon)
- Paper plates, cups, and napkins
- Party snacks (real or pretend: cupcakes, crackers, fruit, cheese, juice boxes)
- Dolls or stuffed animals as guests (4-6)
- Child-sized table or designated gathering space

**Optional**:
- Confetti or pom-poms
- Party music or music player
- Party hat craft supplies
- Simple games (musical chairs, dance scarves)
- Camera to document the party
- Gift boxes or wrapped items

**Cost**: Free to $15 (depending on materials used)

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Gather all materials and organize in accessible locations
2. Clear a large floor area for the party space
3. Set up a planning station at a table with paper and art supplies
4. Place snacks and serving items in a designated area away from crafts
5. Have cleaning supplies ready for after the activity

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (3-5 minutes):
   Introduce the party planning concept by asking exciting questions: "We're going to plan a special party! What kind of party would you like? Who should we invite? What will we serve?" Listen to the child's ideas and incorporate them into the plan. Show the available supplies and let excitement build.

2. **Planning & Decision-Making** (5-10 minutes):
   Help the child make key decisions: party theme (birthday, tea party, dance party), number of guests (keep it to 3-4 for manageability), decorations to use, and simple menu. Model writing a basic invitation together. Support the child in creating invitations for each guest with drawings, colors, and words.

3. **Preparation & Decoration** (8-15 minutes):
   Work together to transform the party space into something special. The child helps hang or arrange decorations, creating a welcoming environment. Create simple party hats, place cards, or table settings if desired. Arrange plates, cups, and napkins in a serving area. Talk about how the space feels special and welcoming.

4. **Hosting & Celebration** (remaining time):
   The child welcomes each guest, greeting them warmly. Practice hospitality by offering refreshments: "Would you like a snack? Here's a napkin." Facilitate conversations between child and guests about the party. Play music, sing happy birthday, dance, or play simple games. Allow the child to lead the celebration naturally.

5. **Wrap-Up** (3-5 minutes):
   When party energy winds down, have the child thank guests and say goodbye with hugs or waves. Reflect together: "What was your favorite part? What did your guests enjoy?" Transition to cleanup by explaining that hosts help restore the space. The child assists with putting away decorations and cleaning up.

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
Simplify significantly by having the adult make most planning decisions while the child participates in choosing. Skip elaborate planning and focus on the celebration itself: decorations, snacks, and dancing. Use pre-made invitations the child colors rather than creating from scratch. Keep the activity to 20-25 minutes with shorter planning (5 min) and longer celebration (15-20 min). Provide substantial adult support throughout, actively hosting most of the party alongside the child.

**3-4+ years**:
Extend the planning phase with written lists and sketches. The child can design and create invitations with words (with adult help for spelling). Explore more sophisticated party themes with matching decorations. Introduce menu planning: "What foods match our theme?" Create place cards with guests' names, plan party games or entertainment, and establish multiple party phases (arrival, food, games, goodbye). Allow the child to take more leadership in hosting and managing the party flow. Duration can extend to 35-45 minutes.

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1: Themed Parties** - Plan different types of parties (garden party, astronaut party, superhero party, pajama party, sports celebration) with matching decorations and snacks

**Variation 2: Holiday Celebrations** - Plan and host seasonal or holiday-specific celebrations using cultural traditions (birthday party, Diwali celebration, Chinese New Year, D√≠a de los Muertos themed party)

**Variation 3: Community Event** - Expand to planning a neighborhood gathering or block party where multiple groups of people are invited

**Repeat play**: Keep supplies organized and visible so the child can independently extend party play throughout the day. Different party themes (dinosaur party, tea party, sports celebration) ensure the activity stays fresh and interesting across multiple sessions.

## üìù Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child feels overwhelmed by too many choices during planning
**Solution**: Limit to 2-3 meaningful choices instead of open-ended questions. For example, ask "Would you like a tea party or a dance party?" rather than "What kind of party?" This reduces decision fatigue while maintaining child agency.

**Challenge**: Child wants to skip planning and jump straight to celebrating
**Solution**: This is developmentally normal and fine. Simplify planning dramatically‚Äî5 minutes of quick decisions, then move to the celebration. Planning skills develop with repeated exposure over time.

**Challenge**: Child becomes frustrated with fine motor demands of creating invitations
**Solution**: Simplify by folding paper with the child drawing one picture and you writing the text. Use picture-based invitations instead of elaborate ones. The planning concept matters more than perfect invitations.

**Challenge**: Too many decorations create an overwhelming, overstimulating space
**Solution**: Use fewer, larger decorations in 2-3 coordinated colors. Simple, calm spaces actually feel more sophisticated and help children function better.

### Follow-Up Activities
1. Create a "party planning" photo album with pictures taken during planning and celebration to reinforce the experience and language
2. Plan a second party with a completely different theme to practice decision-making and creativity
3. Extend hospitality skills by helping plan a real gathering for family members, practicing real hosting responsibilities
