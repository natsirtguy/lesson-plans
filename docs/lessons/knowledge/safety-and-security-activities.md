# ARTS & CULTURE ACTIVITY: Safety and Security Activities

## Activity Summary

Safety and security activities teach young children essential life skills for protecting themselves and recognizing safe vs. unsafe situations through interactive, age-appropriate play and practice. Through this activity, children develop awareness of their environment, learn simple safety rules, practice emergency responses in a non-threatening way, and build confidence in knowing what to do if they need help. These foundational lessons create healthy habits and empower children to make safer choices independently.

**Activity Type**: Practical life skills / Social-emotional learning / Creative role-play

**Duration**: 15-30 minutes (with natural breaks)

**Mess Level**: Low - mostly conversation and movement-based

**Best Environment**: Indoor open space or large table / Outdoor space for some activities / Comfortable, safe setting with few distractions

## Complete Activity Setup

### Materials Needed

**Essential materials**:
- Picture cards or drawings showing safe vs. unsafe situations (8-10 images) - printed or hand-drawn
- Toy telephone (or real phone for practicing) - 1 per child
- First aid kit (toy or real child-safe version) - basic supplies
- Safety signs printable or drawn (stop sign, caution sign, exit sign) - for discussion
- Blanket or cushions - for "safe space" creation
- Dolls or stuffed animals - for practicing care and safety

**Optional enhancements**:
- Emergency resource cards with family contact info (simplified/pictorial)
- Simple uniform pieces or hats (police hat, firefighter hat) - role-play props
- Flashlight - for discussing visibility and safety
- Traffic cones or tape to create paths - for discussing sidewalk safety
- Story books about personal safety (see below)
- Simple puzzle or matching game about safe vs. unsafe choices

**Cost estimate**: Free to $10 (most items are household items or printable; toy phone optional; books from library)

**Preparation time**: 10-15 minutes (gather materials, prepare pictures/printables)

### Step-by-Step Setup

**Before starting**:
1. Gather picture cards showing scenarios (crossing street safely, talking to strangers, what to do if lost, fire safety, etc.)
2. Prepare a comfortable space where conversation and movement can happen
3. Set up toy phone or identify which phone will be used
4. Create "safe space" with blanket or cushions (quiet area)
5. Review the content yourself to ensure it's age-appropriate and non-frightening

**Environment setup**:
- Open, distraction-free space where children can move and sit together
- Easy access to all materials (picture cards, props, toys)
- Safe area for practice activities with no hazards nearby
- Seating arrangements that allow comfortable group conversation
- Calm lighting and quiet background to minimize overstimulation

**Cleaning supplies ready**:
- Paper towels for hands after role-play
- Damp cloth for cleaning shared toys/props
- Hand sanitizer (optional)

## Activity Session Structure

### Opening (2-3 minutes)

**How to introduce and engage**:
- Sit comfortably with the child and introduce the topic gently: "Today we're going to talk about ways to stay safe and healthy. Everyone wants to feel safe, right?"
- Show the concept through a picture or simple scenario: "Have you ever heard of 'safe' and 'unsafe'? Let me show you what I mean."
- Use a familiar example: "When we hold someone's hand to cross the street, that's safe. Stepping into the street without looking is unsafe."
- Make it positive and empowering: "Today you're going to learn some really helpful ways to stay safe!"

**Setting expectations**:
- Emphasize that the activity is learning and play, not scary: "We're going to have fun learning about being safe, like superheroes protecting themselves!"
- Keep tone calm and reassuring: "If anything feels too much, we can take a break or just listen."
- Frame safety as a good thing: "Knowing how to stay safe helps us have more fun because our grown-ups worry less!"

### Main Activity (10-20 minutes)

**Phase 1: Picture-Based Discussion** (5-7 minutes)
Introduce safety concepts through visual learning and conversation.

- Show picture cards one at a time, asking child to identify safe vs. unsafe choices
- Pictures might show: holding an adult's hand while walking, not talking to strangers, knowing emergency number, stop/look/listen before crossing street, washing hands, telling a trusted adult if hurt
- Ask open-ended questions: "What's happening here?" "Is this safe or unsafe?" "Why?"
- Avoid graphic or frightening imagery; keep it simple and matter-of-fact
- Celebrate correct answers: "Yes! That's a safe choice!"
- Comment on and validate the child's thinking: "You noticed that! That's good observing."

**Phase 2: Role-Play and Practice** (5-10 minutes)
Practice safety skills in a playful, manageable way.

- Practice calling for help: Use toy phone or real phone (with adult's number programmed). Child practices saying their name, that they need help, and their location
- "Safe space" practice: Establish a quiet area (under table, with blanket) where child can practice going if scared. Explain: "This is your safe space where you can go if you need quiet time"
- Practice with dolls: Use stuffed animals to practice first aid (putting a band-aid on, getting help for an adult)
- Safety walk: Create a path with tape or cones. Walk it together, stopping to discuss what's safe (walking on sidewalk) vs. unsafe (running into street)
- Meet a "helper" role: Have adult or older sibling play police officer, firefighter, or doctor. Child practices asking for help or telling them a problem

**Phase 3: Free Exploration and Repeat Practice** (remaining time)
Allow child-led engagement with safety concepts.

- Child chooses which safety scenario to practice again: "Do you want to practice calling again, or use the safe space?"
- Use props for extended role-play: If child enjoys the helper role, continue with different scenarios
- Introduce a simple safety rule game: "Can you remember three safe choices? Let's practice them."
- Tell a story together about a character staying safe: "Let's make up a story about a bunny who stays safe"

**Adult role**:
- Stay calm and matter-of-fact; avoid anxiety or fear in your tone
- Model safe behaviors throughout: hold their hand crossing (if outside), look both ways, etc.
- Answer questions honestly but simply: if child asks "What if a stranger takes me?" answer: "That won't happen because grown-ups keep you safe. And you can say 'No!' loudly and run to me"
- Reinforce that safety is everyone's job: "We all help keep each other safe"
- Use positive language: "We DO this" rather than "Don't do that"

### Wrap-Up (3-5 minutes)

**Concluding the activity**:
- Summarize what you learned together: "Today you learned about safe choices. You practiced calling for help and making a safe space!"
- Celebrate their learning: "You're so smart about safety! You remembered to look both ways!"
- Ask a reflection question: "What was your favorite safe thing we practiced?" or "How does it feel to know these safe things?"
- Connect to real life: "Tomorrow when we go to the park, we can practice looking both ways together"

**Transition to next activity**:
- Make cleanup quick and simple: "Can you help put the picture cards back in the folder?"
- Maintain positive tone: "You did such a good job learning about safety today!"
- Offer reassurance if child seems nervous: "Your grown-ups and I keep you safe every day"

## Age Adaptations

### 2-3 years

**Approach**:
- Focus on very basic concepts: holding hands, saying "No," staying close to trusted adult
- Use only 2-3 picture cards to avoid overwhelm
- Keep explanations very simple: "Holding hands keeps you safe when we walk"
- Emphasize physical safety (traffic, falling) more than abstract dangers
- Use lots of repetition; concepts develop through repeated exposure
- Make it playful and fun, not serious or scary
- Practice through everyday situations (holding hand at store, looking before stepping down)
- Use stuffed animals or dolls as main teaching tool—less direct pressure on child

**Duration**: 10-15 minutes (or broken into shorter 5-minute conversations throughout day)

**Adult support**: High - this age needs constant modeling and reminding, not independent understanding. Expect to repeat safety lessons frequently.

### 3-4+ years

**Extensions**:
- Introduce more complex scenarios: what to do if lost in a store, basic fire safety, personal boundaries ("your body is yours")
- Use more picture cards (8-10) covering different domains: traffic, strangers, home safety, emergency numbers
- Practice calling 911 (or local emergency number) with a toy phone; explain when to use it
- Introduce concept of "trusted adults" and who to ask for help: "Safe adults are mom, dad, grandma, teachers..."
- Create a simple safety rule chart together with pictures: "Hold hands when walking," "Tell a grown-up if hurt," "Our safe number is..."
- Role-play more realistic scenarios: "What if we get separated at the park?" "What if someone comes to the door?"
- Teach basic body autonomy: "Nobody touches you in a way that makes you uncomfortable. Tell me if someone does."
- Introduce simple first aid: wash a scrape, get a band-aid, tell a grown-up
- Create a safety treasure hunt: find pictures of safe choices around the house, put sticker on each

**Duration**: They may engage for 20-30 minutes with more complex scenarios and role-play

## Extension Ideas for Repeat Play

**Variation 1: Safety Hunt Around the Home**
Walk through your home together with a checklist of safety items: fire extinguisher location, first aid kit, emergency exit, phone location, safe place to go if scared. Make it an adventure: "Can you find the first aid kit? It keeps us safe!"

**Variation 2: Dress-Up as Safety Helpers**
Provide simple costume pieces (police hat, firefighter vest, doctor stethoscope). Child role-plays being the helper, and you play someone who needs help. Reverses power dynamic in healthy way.

**Variation 3: Create a Family Safety Book**
Work together to draw or print pictures showing your family's safety rules and plans: where everyone meets if separated, emergency numbers, safe rooms in house. Read it together regularly.

**Variation 4: Weather Safety Drama**
Act out scenarios for different weather: tornado (go to safe room), thunderstorm (stay inside), snow (dress warm, stay close), extreme heat (drink water, find shade). Use dramatic play to make learning memorable.

**Variation 5: Community Helper Interviews**
If possible, invite a police officer, firefighter, or EMT to briefly explain their safety role in age-appropriate terms. Children feel more empowered knowing helpers exist in their community.

**Variation 6: Safety Rules Song**
Create a simple tune and sing about safety rules: "Hold your hand, hold your hand, walk safely down the street. Hold your hand, hold your hand, to keep you safe and neat!" Repetition through song aids memory.

**Variation 7: "What If" Game**
Pose simple "what if" scenarios appropriate to age: "What if you see a dog you don't know?" "What if you fall and hurt your knee?" "What if we're separated at the store?" Child answers, you validate, discuss safer options if needed.

**Thematic variations**:
- **Seasonal**: Water safety for summer, fire safety as winter heating increases, bike safety in warmer months
- **Travel-specific**: Airplane safety, car seat safety, stranger awareness when visiting new places
- **New experiences**: First day of school safety rules, visiting doctor/dentist safety, new baby safety (if applicable)
- **Developmental milestones**: Starting to ride a tricycle, learning to swim, going to preschool

## Cultural Context

**Cultural significance**:
Safety awareness is universal—every culture teaches children to protect themselves. However, specific safety priorities vary by culture, geography, and lifestyle. In some cultures, danger awareness and street safety are emphasized early due to high-traffic environments. In others, water safety is paramount in island or river communities. Indigenous cultures often emphasize respect for nature's dangers and learning to coexist safely with wildlife. Teaching safety is an act of love and protection that spans all human societies.

**Exposure to diversity**:
- Discuss safety practices in different cultures: "In some countries, children ride bicycles for transportation, so they learn bike safety early. In others, water crossings are common, so swim safety is priority."
- Show pictures of diverse safety helpers: police officers, firefighters, doctors from different backgrounds and ethnicities
- Read stories about children from different cultures learning to stay safe in their environments
- Acknowledge that some children may have experienced trauma or danger; approach safety teaching with gentleness and awareness
- If your family has specific cultural safety practices, incorporate them: prayer before travel, specific protective rituals, cultural respect for certain situations

- "Fireflies!" by Julie Brinckloe (nature safety and respect)
- "Officer Buckle and Gloria" by Peggy Rathmann (classic about school safety, engaging and funny)
- "The Kissing Hand" by Audrey Penn (separation anxiety and feeling safe)

## Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child becomes anxious or frightened when discussing safety topics
**Why**: Safety discussions can trigger worry, especially if child has experienced scary situations. Abstract thinking about "what ifs" isn't fully developed yet; they may catastrophize.
**Solution**: Stop immediately and reassure. Use very concrete, present-focused language: "Right now, you're safe. I'm here." Avoid hypothetical scenarios for very anxious children. Focus on positive ("Here's what we DO") rather than negative ("Here's what could happen"). Consider spreading safety learning across many conversations rather than one activity. Some children benefit from learning through play without explicit discussion.

**Challenge**: Child seems bored or uninterested in safety discussion
**Why**: Abstract safety concepts don't grab attention like active play. Young children live in the moment and may not relate to "what if" scenarios. Some children are naturally less risk-aware.
**Solution**: Make it more active and playful. Less talking, more doing: role-play, movement, props. Use repetition across many days rather than one long lesson. Incorporate into regular activities: practice safety rules as you do them (hold hand walking, look both ways, etc.) rather than in dedicated sessions. Some children learn safety through lived experience with gentle guidance rather than discussion.

**Challenge**: Child tests boundaries or ignores safety rules immediately after learning them
**Why**: This is completely normal! Young children need MANY repetitions to internalize rules. They test limits to understand consequences. Having heard it once doesn't mean they've learned it.
**Solution**: Expect and accept this. Safety learning is not one-time; it's constant repetition over years. Stay patient and calm. Matter-of-factly re-teach: "Remember, we hold hands when crossing the street." Gently redirect when child forgets. Praise when they follow the rule: "Yes! You looked both ways—that's safe!" Remember that their impulse control is still developing; constant reminders are appropriate, not a sign of failure.

**Challenge**: Child has experienced trauma or has severe anxiety; any safety discussion feels harmful
**Why**: Children who have experienced danger or loss may have heightened fear responses. Discussing safety can trigger traumatic memories or increased anxiety.
**Solution**: Proceed very carefully, in consultation with a therapist or counselor if possible. For these children, safety might be better taught through lived experience (consistently keeping them safe) rather than discussion. Focus on present safety and trust: "Right now, you're safe with me. I keep you safe." Avoid hypotheticals. Be available to discuss fears if child brings them up, but don't initiate safety discussions. Their sense of safety comes from experiencing protective adults, not from fear-based conversations.

**Challenge**: Older sibling says frightening things about dangers, scaring the younger child
**Why**: Older siblings may have learned about fears at school (fire drills, stranger danger) or overheard conversations. They sometimes share this in scary ways to younger siblings.
**Solution**: Have a conversation with the older sibling: "I understand you're thinking about safety, and that's good. But talking about scary things can worry your sibling. Let's talk to parents about safer ways to help them learn." Reassure the younger child in calm, factual terms. Set a family rule: "Scary safety talk is for grown-ups to explain—siblings focus on fun ways to stay safe."

### Tips for Success

- **Keep it age-appropriate**: Focus on tangible, present-moment safety (holding hands, looking both ways) rather than abstract dangers. Young children can't process probability or low-likelihood threats.
- **Model constantly**: Your own safety behaviors (holding the railing, looking before stepping into traffic, telling someone where you're going) teach more than words.
- **Use positive framing**: "We hold hands to stay close" instead of "Don't run away." "We look both ways to stay safe" instead of "You'll get hit by a car."
- **Practice, don't preach**: Teach through doing. When you cross the street together, walk it with intention: "Let's stop. Look both ways. Good—no cars coming. Now we can walk safely."
- **Celebrate effort, not perfection**: Praise when child remembers: "You remembered to hold my hand! That was safe!" Don't punish mistakes.
- **Connect to real life**: Link safety lessons to the child's actual experiences. If they visit the doctor, discuss doctor safety. If learning to swim, practice water safety during lessons.
- **Make safety feel empowering, not fearful**: Frame it as "You're strong and smart and can take care of yourself with help from grown-ups" rather than "Scary things happen."
- **Use props and toys**: Stuffed animals, dolls, and role-play reduce the intensity of abstract discussions and allow children to practice in a safe, playful way.
- **Expect repetition**: Don't expect one lesson to "stick." Safety learning is ongoing throughout childhood, with each year adding complexity.

### What to Do With the Creation

- **Keep a safety plan visible**: If you create a family safety book or rule chart, display it somewhere accessible. Review together regularly (weekly, or before outings).
- **Revisit regularly**: Safety lessons aren't one-and-done. Periodically (every few months or when new situations arise) revisit key safety concepts through play and discussion.
- **Document what they learned**: Note in a journal or take photos of the child demonstrating safety skills (holding hand crossing, practicing emergency call, etc.). Review periodically to see growth.
- **Create a "safety resource" folder**: Keep printed emergency numbers, first aid basics, and safety resources in one place for easy access. Include simple pictures so even young children can point to important information.
- **Share with other caregivers**: Send a note to daycare, preschool, or other caregivers summarizing safety practices you're teaching at home. Consistency across settings helps children learn faster.
- **Adapt as child grows**: As child matures, gradually increase complexity and independence. A 2-year-old learns through modeling; a 4-year-old can practice calling for help; a 5-year-old can understand basic home safety systems.
