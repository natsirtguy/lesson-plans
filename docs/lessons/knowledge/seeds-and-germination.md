# KNOWLEDGE, SKILLS & CULTURE: Seeds and Germination

## Activity Summary

A seed looks dry and dead, but it is actually alive — a tiny plant **embryo** asleep inside a protective coat, packed with its own first meal. In this lesson the child opens a soaked bean seed to find the baby plant hiding inside, then starts a sprout jar and watches **germination** happen with their own eyes over the next few days. The whole lesson is built around one focused idea: a seed already contains the plant, and water and warmth wake it up.

## Vocabulary

**Seed**: A tiny package that holds a baby plant plus a supply of food, wrapped in a protective coat. A seed can wait — sometimes for years — until the conditions are right to grow.

**Embryo**: The baby plant curled up inside the seed, already formed and waiting. When you split a soaked bean in half, the little pale hook you see *is* the embryo — a real, living plant in miniature.

**Germination**: The waking-up of a seed. When a seed drinks water and is warm enough, its cells begin to divide and it starts to grow — this is germination.

**Radicle**: The very first root, and the first part of the embryo to push out of the seed. It grows *downward*, because the seed can sense which way is down (this is called gravitropism — plants feel gravity).

**Cotyledon**: The seed's built-in lunchbox. A bean splits into two fat halves — those are the cotyledons, full of stored food that feeds the embryo until it grows leaves and can make its own food from sunlight.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

The child does two things that directly show the concept: they **open a seed to find the plant inside**, then **start a sprout jar** and watch germination unfold over days. Seeing the embryo makes the abstract idea concrete — the plant isn't created by the soil, it was inside the seed the whole time.

**Activity Type**: Hands-on investigation + multi-day observation

**Duration**: 15-20 minutes (plus 30 seconds a day for a week to check the jar)

**Setup time**: The night before — soak a handful of dried beans in water for 8-12 hours

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- A handful of large dried beans (lima, kidney, or pinto) — soaked overnight in water
- A clear glass jar or a zip bag
- Paper towels or cotton balls
- Water

**Optional**:
- A magnifying glass to look at the embryo
- Tape and a sunny window (to stick a bag up and watch)
- A second jar to try a "no water" comparison

**Cost**: Free / under $5

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. The night before, soak a small handful of dried beans in a bowl of water for 8-12 hours so they swell and soften.
2. Set aside 2-3 soaked beans for opening, and keep the rest for the sprout jar.
3. Dampen a few paper towels (wet, not dripping).

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 min):
   Hold up a *dry* bean and a *soaked* bean. "This one is dry and hard. This one drank water all night and got fat and soft. A seed isn't a rock — there's a tiny living baby plant inside, called an embryo. Let's find it."

2. **Open the seed** (4-5 min):
   Help the child peel the soft coat off a soaked bean, then gently split it into two halves along its natural seam. Point out the small pale hook nestled inside: "That's the embryo — a real baby plant! This little point is the radicle, its first root. And these two big soft halves are the cotyledons — that's the food the baby plant eats until it grows leaves." Use the magnifying glass to look closely.

3. **Start the sprout jar** (5-6 min):
   Line the inside of a clear jar with a damp paper towel. Tuck 3-4 soaked beans *between the glass and the towel* so they're visible from outside. Add a little water at the bottom to keep the towel damp. Set it somewhere warm and light. "Now we wait. Water and warmth will wake these seeds up — that's germination. Watch the glass every day."

4. **Wrap-up** (2-3 min):
   "The plant was inside the seed the whole time. It just needed water and warmth to wake up. In a few days the radicle — the first root — will push out and grow *down*. Let's check it every morning." Optionally start a second jar with *no* water to compare later.

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
- You do the peeling and splitting; the child watches and touches the halves
- Focus on two words used over and over: "seed" and "wake up"
- The daily jar check is the main event — short and exciting

**3-4+ years**:
- Let the child split the bean and find the embryo themselves
- Introduce "gravitropism": the root always grows down, the shoot always grows up, even if you turn the jar. Test it by rotating the jar once a sprout appears.
- Keep a simple picture diary: draw the jar each day
- Try the no-water comparison and ask why one grew and one didn't

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1 — Turn the jar upside down**: Once a root is growing down, gently rotate the jar 90°. Over the next day the root curves to grow downward again — the seed "knows" which way is down.

**Variation 2 — Different seeds race**: Sprout beans, peas, and sunflower seeds side by side. Which wakes up first?

**Repeat play**: Once sprouted, plant one seedling in soil and keep watching it grow into a real plant — connecting germination to the whole life cycle.

## Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: The bean is hard to split or the embryo tears.
**Solution**: Soak longer (a full 12 hours). Split gently along the natural seam with your thumbnail. Even a torn one shows the two cotyledons and the little root point.

**Challenge**: Nothing sprouts after several days.
**Solution**: Check that the towel stayed damp (not dry, not flooded) and the jar was warm. Beans usually show a root in 2-4 days. Old beans sometimes won't sprout — start extras so at least one works.

### Follow-Up Activities
1. Eat a snack of foods that are seeds — beans, peas, corn, sunflower seeds — "we eat the plant's lunchbox!"
2. On a walk, look for seeds: maple "helicopters," dandelion puffs, acorns. Talk about how each one carries a plant inside.
3. Once a seedling has leaves, explain it now makes its own food from sunlight (a gentle bridge to photosynthesis).
