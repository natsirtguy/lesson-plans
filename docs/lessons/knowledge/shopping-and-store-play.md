## üéØ Activity Summary

Children set up and operate a simple store, taking on roles as shopkeeper and customer to explore buying and selling concepts. Through this imaginative economic play, children practice money handling, develop social interaction skills, and build math understanding while engaging in natural, playful learning.

## Vocabulary

**Supply and Demand**: The relationship between how much of something is available (supply) and how many people want it (demand). When lots of people want something but there isn't much of it, the price goes up. When there's plenty and fewer people want it, the price goes down ‚Äî this invisible push-and-pull determines almost every price you see.

**Scarcity**: When there isn't enough of something for everyone who wants it. Scarcity is the fundamental problem of economics ‚Äî there are limited resources but unlimited wants. That's why we have to make choices about what to buy.

**Profit**: The money left over after a shopkeeper pays for the items they sell. If a shopkeeper buys apples for 2 cents each and sells them for 5 cents, the profit is 3 cents. Profit is why people open stores ‚Äî it's their reward for providing things others need.

**Medium of Exchange**: Something that people agree to use as money ‚Äî it could be coins, shells, beads, or paper bills. Before money existed, people had to barter (trade item for item), which only worked if each person had exactly what the other wanted. Money solves this problem by being universally accepted.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

Children explore buying and selling through hands-on pretend play. This activity builds math skills (counting money), social-emotional development (turn-taking and role-playing), language development, and understanding of economic exchanges in a natural, playful context.

**Activity Type**: Dramatic play, role-play, hands-on learning

**Duration**: 20-35 minutes (with natural breaks)

**Setup time**: 10-15 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- Play money or real coins (pennies/dimes) - enough for multiple transactions
- Items to "sell" (small toys, plastic food items, books, blocks) - 8-12 items
- Small bags or baskets for shopping - 2-3 bags
- Price tags or labels (made from index cards and tape)
- A "counter" (low table, shelf, or designated area) for store display
- Small box or container as a cash register

**Optional**:
- Receipt book or paper for writing receipts
- Toy cash register with coins
- Apron or name tag for shopkeeper role
- Shopping list template or paper
- Simple calculator or counting tool
- Decorations (signs, posters)

**Cost**: Free to Under $5 (uses household items)

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Gather safe items to be "sold" that interest the child (choose 8-12 objects)
2. Assign simple prices (1-5 cents for younger children, up to 25 cents for older)
3. Create and attach clear price tags to each item
4. Arrange items on display surface at child's eye level
5. Set up a "counter" area separate from the shopping display

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 min):
   Introduce the store and the economics behind it: "We're going to run a real store! Stores exist because of something called *scarcity* ‚Äî nobody can make everything they need by themselves. So people specialize: one person grows apples, another bakes bread, and they trade. But trading directly is hard ‚Äî what if the apple grower doesn't want bread? That's why people invented *money* ‚Äî a medium of exchange that everyone agrees to accept."

2. **Exploration** (3-5 min):
   Set up items with different prices and ask: "Why do you think this toy costs more than this block?" Introduce the concept: "When something is *scarce* ‚Äî there's only one of it ‚Äî people want it more, so the price goes up. When there are lots of something, the price goes down. That's called *supply and demand*." Let the child examine items and predict which ones should cost more based on how rare or desirable they are.

3. **Discovery** (10-15 min):
   Run the store with real economic principles. The shopkeeper must calculate *profit*: "I bought these items for 2 cents each. If I sell them for 5 cents, I keep 3 cents profit ‚Äî that's how shopkeepers earn a living." Introduce scarcity experiments: start with 5 of one item, sell them, then announce "Only 1 left!" and ask "Should the price go up or down now that it's scarce?" Let the child experience the *demand* side: "I only have 5 pennies ‚Äî I want all these things but I can't afford them all. I have to make a *choice*. That's what scarcity means ‚Äî not enough for everything, so we must decide what matters most."

4. **Wrap-up** (3-5 min):
   Count the shopkeeper's profits together: "We started with 10 cents of items and earned 20 cents ‚Äî that's 10 cents profit!" Ask: "What happened when we had only one toy left? Why did the price change?" Reflect on the key concepts: "Today we learned that prices aren't random ‚Äî they come from supply and demand. And money exists because bartering was too hard."

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
Use fewer items (5-7) with simple prices (1-2 cents). Focus on the core exchange: "You give money, I give the item ‚Äî that's a *transaction*." Demonstrate scarcity concretely: have only one of a popular item and say "It's *scarce* ‚Äî only one left! Everyone wants it." Use real coins for sensory engagement. Keep profit simple: "I paid 1 cent, I sell for 2 cents, I keep 1 cent extra ‚Äî that's *profit*."

**3-4+ years**:
Run full supply-and-demand experiments. Start the day with items at one price, then change prices based on demand: "Everyone wants the red car ‚Äî the price goes up to 10 cents!" Introduce *competition*: set up two stores and see how competing sellers lower prices to attract customers. Calculate profit at session end. Discuss *opportunity cost*: "If you spend all your money on the car, you can't buy the book ‚Äî what's more important to you?" Introduce the concept of *barter* by trying to trade without money and discovering the "double coincidence of wants" problem ‚Äî both people must want exactly what the other has. Let the child set prices and discover what customers will pay.

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1: Thematic Store**
Create a specialized store based on the child's interests: grocery store (plastic food items), toy store, library (books for checkout), restaurant (food items for ordering), pet store (stuffed animals), or clothing store (dress-up items).

**Variation 2: Customer Challenges**
Add simple scenarios that extend problem-solving: "I want to buy 2 toys but only have 5 cents" or "What can I get with just 2 coins?" These create natural math and social negotiation opportunities.

**Repeat play**:
Rotate items in and out of the store every week to maintain freshness. Create seasonal variations: holiday store, back-to-school store, farmer's market, or garage sale. Keep the setup available for child-initiated play during free time; many children will return to it independently.

## üìù Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child gets stuck in one role and resists switching
**Solution**: Frame role changes as exciting transitions. Model the new role enthusiastically. If resistance continues, play with two different areas so both roles feel equally appealing. Never force a switch; gradual transitions work better.

**Challenge**: Child wants to keep taking money without understanding transactions
**Solution**: Use simple language: "You give me the money, I give you the toy." Use fewer coins (1-2) per transaction initially. Practice the exchange motion repeatedly and celebrate understanding when it happens.

**Challenge**: Disputes over who gets to be the shopkeeper
**Solution**: Use a timer for role rotations: "You're the shopkeeper for 5 minutes, then we switch." Create equally appealing roles: "The shopkeeper decides what to sell AND the customer gets to choose what to buy - both are important!" Have multiple roles available: shopkeeper, cashier, customer, store helper.

**Challenge**: Child loses interest quickly
**Solution**: Introduce new items regularly to refresh the store. Add complications when interest wanes: "What if everything costs 2 pennies?" Add a second player. Make it more dramatic with a story or sound effects. Remember that 15 minutes of engaged play is success for younger toddlers.

### Follow-Up Activities

1. **Real shopping trips**: Visit a local store together and talk about what shopkeepers and customers are doing. Let the child observe actual transactions.

2. **Money exploration**: Practice counting coins during playtime, sorting by value, or talking about how coins are used in everyday life.

3. **Store variations**: Set up different themed stores throughout the month (farmer's market, library, restaurant) to explore how commerce works in different contexts.
