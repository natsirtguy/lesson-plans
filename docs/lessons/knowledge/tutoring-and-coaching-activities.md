## üéØ Activity Summary

Tutoring and coaching activities involve a child providing one-on-one guidance to help another child develop a specific skill or work through a learning challenge. This teaches both children valuable skills: the tutor gains confidence and communication abilities while the learner receives personalized support, building understanding that people learn in different ways and at different paces.

## Vocabulary

**Model**: To demonstrate or show someone how to do something by doing it yourself first. When you model how to fit puzzle pieces, the learner watches and learns by seeing your actions.

**Encourage**: To give someone confidence and support so they feel brave enough to try something new. When you say "Good trying!" even if someone doesn't succeed, you are encouraging them.

**Persist**: To keep trying even when something is difficult or doesn't work the first time. When you practice throwing a ball over and over until you get better, you are persisting.

**Strategies**: Different methods or ways of doing something to solve a problem or learn a skill. Teaching strategies might include showing slowly, breaking the task into small steps, or trying a different way.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

In this activity, one child takes on the role of coach or tutor to help another child learn a specific skill. Through guided practice and encouragement, both children develop important abilities: the tutor learns patience, communication, and teaching skills while the learner gets personalized support and builds confidence in trying new things.

**Activity Type**: Teaching activity / Peer learning / Coaching and support / Social-emotional development

**Duration**: 10-20 minutes per coaching session (flexible based on task)

**Setup time**: 5-10 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- Specific materials related to the skill being taught (blocks, puzzle, ball, toy, art supplies)
- Space for learner and tutor to sit or stand comfortably together
- Optional instruction cards or visual guides to help the tutor remember steps

**Optional**:
- "Tutor badge" or special hat to mark the coaching role
- Checklist of coaching tips for the tutor to reference
- Reward stickers or simple acknowledgment chart to celebrate progress
- Mirror or camera so learner can see themselves (helpful for physical skills)
- Multiple variations of materials for practicing at different difficulty levels

**Cost**: Free (using available materials; optional items under $5)

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Choose a specific, achievable skill (fitting puzzle pieces, throwing a ball, building an arch with blocks)
2. Identify which child will be the tutor/coach
3. Brief the tutor on the skill and coaching strategies: encourage, show, help, celebrate
4. Gather all materials needed and choose a quiet space with few distractions
5. Ensure both children are rested and ready

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (1-2 minutes):
   Introduce the tutor and learner: "Today, [Tutor] is going to help [Learner] learn something new. What are we working on?"
   - Have the tutor explain what skill they'll work on together
   - Ask the learner: "Are you ready to practice? What do you want to learn?"
   - Build excitement: "This is going to be fun! [Tutor] is really good at this."
   - Set expectations: "It's okay to make mistakes‚Äîthat's how we learn."

2. **Demonstration** (2-3 minutes):
   The tutor shows the skill clearly and step-by-step.
   - Tutor demonstrates slowly and narrates: "Watch me first. I turn the piece this way... then it fits like this."
   - Points out key steps: "See how I check which way it goes before I push?"
   - Does it again, keeping language simple and clear
   - Asks the learner: "Did you see how I did that?"

3. **Guided Practice** (4-8 minutes):
   The learner practices with support from the tutor.
   - Learner attempts the skill while tutor watches and encourages
   - Tutor offers specific feedback: "Good! You're really close. Try turning it a little more."
   - Tutor helps physically if needed: "Let me help guide your hand. Feel how it turns? Now you try."
   - Break the task into smaller steps if the learner struggles
   - Celebrate attempts and effort, not just success: "I see you trying! That's the hard part."

4. **Wrap-up** (2-3 minutes):
   - Give a time warning: "One more try, and then we'll stop for today"
   - Celebrate the learner's effort: "You really learned a lot today! You tried so hard."
   - Thank the tutor: "[Tutor], you were such a great helper. You explained it really clearly."
   - Ask reflection questions: "What was the easiest part? What felt hard?"
   - Both children help put materials away

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
At this age, tutoring is very simple and adult-guided. Choose extremely simple skills (rolling a ball, turning a page, stacking two blocks). The tutor is more of a "buddy" than a teacher‚Äîthey do it alongside the learner. Adult guides both children through parallel practice. Keep sessions very short (5-10 minutes) with multiple attempts and celebration of any effort.

**3-4+ years**:
Choose more complex skills (puzzle strategy, throwing accuracy, building specific structures). The tutor can remember and explain multiple steps without constant adult prompting. Introduce problem-solving coaching: "What could you try next if that doesn't work?" Offer variations and reflective questions. Tutor and learner can sustain longer sessions (15-20 minutes) with multiple rounds of practice.

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1**: Skill Progression - Continue coaching the same skill over multiple days, adding complexity each time. Start with basic ball throw, progress to accuracy targets. Builds mastery and shows growth over time.

**Variation 2**: Peer Teaching Pairs - Form consistent tutoring pairs where the same two children work together on different skills. Builds relationship and consistency.

**Variation 3**: Video Review - Record the tutoring session and review it together. Children see their own progress, which is powerful motivation.

**Variation 4**: Curriculum-Based Coaching - Connect coaching to structured learning: letter recognition coaching, number practice, color sorting. Makes tutoring intentional while keeping it relational.

**Themed variations**:
- Sports coaching: throwing, kicking, balancing, coordination
- Art coaching: brushwork, cutting, building complex structures
- Academic coaching: puzzle solving, letter/number recognition, sequencing
- Life skills coaching: putting on shoes, opening containers, utensil use

## üìù Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Tutor becomes frustrated or impatient with learner
**Solution**: Coach the tutor beforehand: "Everyone learns at their own speed. Your job is to keep trying new ways to help." Give the tutor specific strategies: "If that way didn't work, try showing it differently." Keep sessions short so frustration doesn't build. Celebrate the tutor's patience: "I noticed you showed her three times. That's what good teachers do!"

**Challenge**: Learner gets frustrated or gives up quickly
**Solution**: Break the skill into much smaller steps. Celebrate every attempt: "You tried! That's the important part." Help the learner experience small successes early. Take breaks and come back. Normalize mistakes: "Everyone messes up when learning. That's normal!"

**Challenge**: Learner copies the tutor's movements but doesn't understand the skill
**Solution**: This is developmentally normal‚Äîimitation is an important learning stage! Ask questions to build understanding: "Why did I turn it that way?" Keep practicing; understanding will develop over time.

**Challenge**: Tutor does the activity for the learner instead of coaching them
**Solution**: Gently redirect: "Let them try. Your job is to show and encourage, not to do it for them." Give the tutor specific coaching language: "Say 'you try' or 'good try!'" Start with easy activities where the learner can succeed quickly.

### Follow-Up Activities

1. **Reverse Roles**: Have the learner teach the tutor something that they learned first, reinforcing their understanding and building confidence.

2. **Video Documentation**: Record brief clips of the tutoring sessions to celebrate progress and show children how they've improved over time.

3. **Family Teaching Traditions**: Invite family members to coach children in cultural skills or family traditions, expanding the circle of teachers and learners.
