# PHYSICAL ACTIVITY: Outdoor Survival Skills Games

## Activity Summary

Outdoor survival skills games introduces young children to practical outdoor competencies through playful activities, building problem-solving abilities, resourcefulness, and confidence in natural environments. This activity combines gross motor play with cognitive challenges as children learn basic skills like gathering materials, building shelters, creating fire safely, and navigating outdoor challenges. Through games and play-based learning, children develop independence, creative thinking, and a sense of competence in outdoor situations.

**Activity Type**: Outdoor adventure / Skill-based learning / Problem-solving play

**Duration**: 25-45 minutes (including setup and game time)

**Energy Level**: Moderate to High

**Best Environment**: Park, nature area, backyard, campground, or open field with natural materials

**Space Required**: Large open area (minimum 50x50 feet) with access to natural materials and varied terrain

## Complete Activity Structure

### Equipment Needed

**Essential items**:
- Natural materials (sticks, rocks, leaves, branches, logs)
- Rope or paracord for shelter/tying activities
- Containers for collecting water or materials
- Whistle or bell
- Water and snacks
- First aid kit

**Optional enhancements**:
- Compass for navigation challenges
- Map for route-finding activities
- Binoculars for observation
- Field guides for plant/animal identification
- Fire-starting materials (matches, kindling) for supervised safe fire activity
- Tarp or waterproof material for shelter building
- Rope for climbing or knot practice
- Backpack for carrying materials
- Camera for documenting survival challenges

**DIY alternatives**:
- Use only natural materials found at location (free)
- Improvise containers from available materials
- Use walking stick as compass direction trainer
- Make simple map from memory of area
- Create shelter from materials found on-site
- Use existing structures (trees, rocks) as shelter base

**Cost estimate**: Free (using natural materials only) to Under $20

**Safety equipment**:
- First aid kit
- Whistle for emergency signal
- Water bottles
- Sun protection (hat, sunscreen)
- Appropriate footwear

### Space Setup

**Before starting**:
1. Scout area thoroughly and identify:
   - Safe areas for different activities (shelter building, fire, gathering)
   - Available natural materials (sticks, branches, leaves, rocks, water source)
   - Hazards (holes, sharp objects, unstable terrain, animal habitats)
   - Weather exposure (sun, wind, water)
2. Establish activity stations for different survival skills
3. Create clear boundaries for exploration
4. Identify safe water source or have water brought in
5. Plan emergency shelter location if needed
6. Brief child on expectations and rules

**Activity stations**:
1. **Shelter building area**: Open space with available sticks, branches, leaves
2. **Fire safety area**: Safe location away from vegetation (if doing fire activity)
3. **Material gathering area**: Area rich with sticks, rocks, leaves, natural items
4. **Navigation area**: Open area with landmarks for orientation challenges
5. **Water finding area**: Access to water source or safe area for water skill games
6. **Rest and reflection area**: Shaded spot with water and snacks

**Boundaries**:
- Mark safe exploration area
- Designate out-of-bounds zones (cliffs, deep water, private property)
- Use natural markers (stream, fence line) or temporary markers (rope, cones)
- Establish check-in system: regular accountability on location

**Safety considerations**:
- **Fire safety** (if using fire): Adult-controlled, safe distance established, extinguishing plan
- **Sharp or unsafe materials**: Avoid handling broken glass, sharp metal, thorny plants
- **Water safety**: Prevent drowning risk; only allow water activities in shallow areas with close supervision
- **Exposure**: Sun, weather changes, temperature extremes
- **Getting lost**: Clear boundaries and regular check-ins; teach child to stay visible
- **Insect and animal hazards**: Know local risks (venomous insects, dangerous animals)
- **Overexertion**: Monitor for fatigue; include frequent rest breaks
- **Allergic reactions**: Be aware of plant allergies in area; know what to avoid

## Session Flow

### Warm-Up (3-5 minutes)

**Purpose**: Prepare body for activities; build excitement about survival skills; establish learning mindset

**Warm-up activities**:
1. **Survival scenario introduction** - Tell a simple story: "Imagine we're explorers on a camping trip. We need to find materials to build a shelter. Can you help me? Let's get our bodies ready!"
2. **Movement exploration** - Practice movements needed for survival activities: bending (picking up materials), reaching (gathering sticks), climbing, walking over terrain
3. **Sensory awareness** - Practice noticing surroundings: "What do you see? Listen to the sounds. Smell the air. We're going to be survival detectives!"

**Warm-up cues**: "Today we're going to learn survival skills! We'll build shelters, find materials, and solve outdoor challenges. Are you ready to be a survival expert?"

### Main Activity (18-35 minutes)

#### Introduction & Demonstration (2-3 minutes)

**Setup**:
- Explain the survival scenario: "Pretend we're camping. We need to build a shelter. We need to find water. We need to stay safe. Are we ready?"
- Demonstrate one simple skill: Show gathering sticks, building simple shelter framework, or safe knot
- Explain game rules and expectations
- Ask child what they know about survival: "What do you think we need to survive outside?"

**Coaching point**:
- "Survival means using what nature gives us to stay safe and healthy"

#### Skill Practice (3-5 minutes)

**Skill breakdown**:
- **Material identification**: Identifying suitable sticks, rocks, leaves for different purposes
- **Gathering technique**: Collecting materials safely without breaking things unnecessarily
- **Building basics**: Leaning sticks against trees, creating simple frameworks
- **Problem-solving**: "This branch is too thin. What can we use instead?"

**Coaching cues**:
- "Look for branches that are strong and straight"
- "We use what nature gives us; we don't waste"
- "Good problem-solving! You found a way to make that work"

#### Guided Survival Skills Games (12-25 minutes)

**Game 1: Material Gathering Challenge** (5-7 minutes)
- **Objective**: Gather materials for survival task
- **How to play**: Give child a list (verbal or picture-based) of items to find: sticks of different sizes, smooth rocks, leaves, stones
- **Challenge variations**:
  - Find items for building (strong sticks, branches)
  - Find items for fire (dry kindling, bark)
  - Find items for decoration or tools (unique rocks, shaped branches)
- **Success**: Child gathers items and understands their use

**Game 2: Shelter Building Challenge** (5-7 minutes)
- **Objective**: Build a simple shelter using gathered materials
- **How to play**: Help child construct a framework using sticks against tree or structure
- **Variations**:
  - Simple lean-to: Lean sticks against low branch or tree
  - Stick teepee: Arrange sticks in cone shape; cover with leaves
  - Branch fort: Collect large branches; create semi-enclosed space
- **Success**: Child creates structure that could provide basic shelter

**Game 3: Fire Safety and Preparation** (5-7 minutes)
- **Objective**: Learn fire safety and prepare materials (if appropriate for location)
- **How to play**:
  - Teach fire safety: respect, proper distance, adult supervision
  - Collect kindling, tinder, and firewood (size appropriate)
  - Practice fire ring setup using rocks
  - (Optional with close adult supervision) Build and light small fire
- **Safety rule**: Child never handles fire directly; adult only with supervision
- **Success**: Child understands fire safety and can prepare materials

**Game 4: Navigation and Orientation Challenge** (5-7 minutes)
- **Objective**: Navigate using landmarks and directions
- **How to play**:
  - Hide an object (toy, flag, marker) in designated area
  - Give child directions using cardinal directions or landmarks: "Walk toward the big tree, then look left"
  - Child follows directions to find object
  - Role reversal: Child hides object; adult follows their directions
- **Variations**:
  - Compass challenge: Use compass to find directions
  - Landmark memory: Walk route; return with eyes closed (guided)
- **Success**: Child can navigate using directions and landmarks

**Game 5: Water Finding and Safety** (5-7 minutes)
- **Objective**: Find water and practice safe water skills
- **How to play**:
  - If natural water present: Locate water source; discuss water safety
  - Demonstrate safe water collection method (using containers, not drinking directly)
  - Discuss water purification (boiling, filtering) in age-appropriate way
  - Practice water transport: Carry container from water source
- **If no natural water**: Bury container; have child "find" it; practice retrieval
- **Safety**: Emphasize never drinking untreated water
- **Success**: Child understands water importance and basic safety

**Game 6: Knot Tying and Lashing** (5-7 minutes)
- **Objective**: Learn basic knots for securing shelter and materials
- **How to play**:
  - Show simple knots: square knot, bow, half-hitch
  - Practice on practice rope or cord
  - Use knots to secure shelter materials
  - Challenge: Tie bundle of sticks together
- **Variations**:
  - Knot races: How fast can you tie this knot?
  - Knot strength test: Which knot holds tightest?
  - Decorative knots: Create rope art or decoration
- **Success**: Child can tie simple knots and use them purposefully

#### Free Exploration (3-5 minutes)
- Allow child to explore camp area and work on survival challenges independently
- Observe their approach to problem-solving
- Ask guiding questions rather than directing

### Cool-Down (3-5 minutes)

**Purpose**: Transition from active skills practice to calm reflection

**Cool-down activities**:
1. **Sit together in shelter** - If shelter was built, sit inside to appreciate the creation
2. **Reflection circle** - Ask: "What survival skill did you like best? What was hard? What did you learn?"
3. **Pack up camp** - Work together to gather materials and restore area
4. **Celebration** - "You learned so many survival skills today! You'd make a great outdoor explorer!"

**Transition**: "We had an exciting survival skills adventure. Now let's rest and think about what we learned."

## Age Adaptations

### 2-3 years

**Modifications**:
- **Complexity**: Very simple activities only—gathering materials, basic shelter building
- **Independence**: Adult-led with significant participation; not independent play
- **Skills focus**: Gathering, basic stacking/building, following directions
- **Safety**: Close supervision; adult handles any tools, fire, or water
- **Duration**: 20-30 minutes total activity
- **Expectations**: Focus on participation and fun; not on creating "real" survival structures

**Adult support**:
- Demonstrate and then guide child's participation
- Hold materials while child places them
- Do complex tasks while child watches and learns
- Hand over simple parts: "Can you put this stick here?"
- Provide constant encouragement and simple explanations

**Success looks like**: Child participates in gathering and building with adult help; shows interest in materials and structures.

### 3-4+ years

**Challenges to add**:
- **Independence**: Child can gather materials with minimal direction
- **Complexity**: Can create more sturdy structures; understand structural concepts
- **Skill depth**: Learning proper knots, fire safety, navigation
- **Problem-solving**: "What materials work best? How can we make it stronger?"
- **Duration**: 30-45 minutes with variety of activities
- **Safety understanding**: Can learn and follow safety rules; understand why rules matter
- **Teaching others**: Can explain to younger child what they learned

**Duration**: Can extend to 45+ minutes with multiple skill stations

**Extensions**:
- Multi-day camping trips applying survival skills
- Learning more advanced skills: fire-starting, water purification, rope work
- Building more complex shelters in different environments
- Navigation challenges with maps and compass
- Survival games with teams and competition elements

## Extension Ideas

### Progressive Skill Development
- **Week 1**: Learn gathering and basic building
- **Week 2**: Add fire safety and preparation
- **Week 3**: Introduce navigation and orientation
- **Week 4**: Add knot tying and shelter reinforcement
- **Ongoing**: Combine skills in multi-skill challenges

### Real-World Application Projects
- **Backyard camping**: Apply survival skills to actual camping experience
- **Outdoor cooking**: Use gathered wood and safe fire for cooking
- **Extended shelter stays**: Spend extended time in built shelter
- **Multi-day expeditions**: Use skills on longer outdoor adventures

### Challenge Progressions
- **Scenario games**: "We're lost in the woods. What do we do?" scenarios
- **Timed challenges**: Complete survival tasks within time limits
- **Team challenges**: Work with other children on survival games
- **Variation games**: Build shelter in different locations (flat ground, near tree, in field)

### Documentation and Planning
- **Survival journal**: Draw or describe survival activities and learning
- **Skill checklist**: Track which skills have been learned
- **Planning expeditions**: Plan camping trip applying survival skills
- **Teach others**: Teach family member a survival skill

## Safety and Supervision

### Pre-Activity Safety Checklist
- [ ] Area scouted for hazards
- [ ] Natural materials safe to handle (no toxins, splinters, sharp edges)
- [ ] Fire area safe and prepared (if doing fire activity)
- [ ] Water source safe and accessible
- [ ] First aid kit present
- [ ] Child has water and appropriate clothing
- [ ] Sunscreen applied and reapply plan established
- [ ] Someone knows location and expected return time
- [ ] Weather appropriate for outdoor activity
- [ ] Child briefed on rules and expectations

### Supervision During Activity

**Constant supervision required**:
- Near water or fire
- During any climbing or rope activities
- In unfamiliar terrain
- For all children under 3
- When using tools or sharp materials

**Nearby supervision**:
- During material gathering (can be within sight)
- During shelter building (observing and assisting as needed)
- During games (close enough to intervene if needed)
- Frequent check-ins: "Where are you? What are you doing?"

### Hazard-Specific Safety

**Material gathering safety**:
- **Thorny plants**: Know and avoid poisonous/thorny plants in area
- **Splinters**: Check materials for large splinters; smooth if needed
- **Sharp edges**: Avoid glass, metal, or other sharp materials
- **Dead branches**: Use caution with dead branches; they may be brittle
- **Heavy materials**: Ensure child isn't carrying materials too heavy

**Shelter building safety**:
- **Structural stability**: Ensure shelter won't collapse; don't allow climbing on unstable structures
- **Falling materials**: Check that materials won't fall on child
- **Enclosed spaces**: Provide adequate ventilation; child can exit easily
- **Crush hazard**: Careful if stacking heavy materials

**Fire safety** (if fire activity included):
- **Safe distance**: Minimum 6-10 feet from flames
- **Adult control**: Only adult handles fire; child observes
- **Clear area**: Remove flammable materials around fire
- **Water source**: Extinguishing method ready at all times
- **Embers**: Child never approaches fire after it's lit
- **Hand safety**: Use tools, never bare hands near fire
- **Wind**: Avoid fire activity in high wind

**Navigation activity safety**:
- **Boundaries**: Clear boundaries for search area
- **Getting lost**: Keep area compact; child stays visible
- **Time limit**: Set specific time limit for finding object
- **Backup plan**: Know where child is if they go off-course

**Water activity safety** (if applicable):
- **Depth**: Only in shallow water where child can stand; never allow deep water
- **Staying dry**: Decide if child will get wet; plan accordingly
- **Temperature**: Water temperature must be safe
- **Adult presence**: Adult in or immediately adjacent to water
- **Exit plan**: Clear way for child to exit water
- **Floating debris**: Avoid water with debris or hazardous materials

### Injury Prevention and Response

**Common survival activity injuries**:
- **Splinters**: Inevitable with gathering; remove and continue if willing
- **Scraped knees/hands**: Clean and bandage; monitor for infection
- **Sunburn**: Use sunscreen; limit sun exposure
- **Insect bites**: Use insect repellent; don't let child scratch
- **Dehydration**: Frequent water breaks; monitor for signs

**Prevention**:
- Proper supervision and spotting
- Clear safety rules and enforcement
- Teaching proper technique
- Monitoring for fatigue
- Frequent water and rest breaks
- Appropriate activity level for child's age

**Response**:
1. Stop activity for injuries
2. Assess and provide first aid
3. Determine if activity can continue
4. Watch for complications
5. Seek medical attention for serious injuries

## Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Encouragement & Coaching Tips

**What to say**:
- "You're learning real survival skills! You're becoming an outdoor expert."
- "Look at what you created—that's a real shelter! You're resourceful."
- "Good problem-solving. You found a way to make that work with what you had."
- "You're learning to be independent and self-reliant. That's wonderful."
- "I'm proud of how hard you worked today. You accomplished so much!"

**What to avoid**:
- Don't make survival skills seem scary; keep it fun and playful
- Don't force child to handle things they're uncomfortable with (fire, deep water)
- Don't dismiss concerns about animals or safety; address them respectfully
- Don't compare child's survival abilities to other children
- Don't expect adult-level competence; this is learning and play
- Don't rush; let child explore and experiment

**When to help**:
- Offer guidance when child is stuck: "What could we use instead?"
- Provide safety enforcement immediately
- Help with complex tasks while child watches and learns
- Step in if child becomes frustrated or overwhelmed
- Supervise all fire and water activities closely
- Intervene for safety before allowing unsafe attempts

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: Child is scared of animals, insects, or other nature elements

**Why**: Young children may have legitimate fears about outdoor elements. These are often protective and normal.

**Solution**:
1. Never force exposure to scary elements
2. Normalize feelings: "Some people feel scared of bugs. That's okay."
3. Provide information: "Spiders help keep bad bugs away. They're actually helpful."
4. Observe from distance: "Let's watch from here where you feel safe"
5. Build familiarity gradually: Repeated safe exposure often reduces fear
6. Provide comfort: "I'm here. You're safe."

**Timeline**: Fears usually decrease with positive experiences and age. Some children remain cautious (which is healthy). Never push past genuine fear.

---

**Challenge**: Child is scared of fire or refuses fire activity

**Why**: Fear of fire is a healthy protective response. Young children don't fully understand fire danger and may be appropriately cautious.

**Solution**:
1. Never force fire activities
2. Explain fire safety clearly: "Fire is hot and can hurt us. We watch from far away."
3. Start very simple: Let child observe adult-controlled fire from safe distance
4. Build comfort gradually: "Fire keeps us warm. Fire cooks food. We respect fire."
5. Skip fire entirely: Survival can be taught without actual fire
6. Alternative: Use pretend fire or camp stove to teach cooking

**Timeline**: Some children develop comfort with fire over time. Others may never enjoy it (and that's fine). Respect child's comfort level.

---

**Challenge**: Child doesn't want to build shelter or thinks it's "boring"

**Why**: Young children may not see the point of survival skills. They want to play, not work at building.

**Solution**:
1. Make it fun: "We're making a fort! A secret hideout! A dragons' den!"
2. Use imagination: "This is our camping home! Should we decorate it?"
3. Use it: Spend time inside the created shelter; it becomes worthwhile
4. Simplify: Don't require "real" shelters; simple structures are fine
5. Make it a game: Race to build, see who can build tallest, etc.
6. Join in: Build together; your enthusiasm is contagious

**Timeline**: Understanding purpose of survival skills develops over time. Young children need lots of playfulness and imagination to stay engaged.

---

**Challenge**: Child won't participate or wants to leave

**Why**: Activity may be too complex, too long, or not matching child's interests. Some children are simply less interested in survival skills.

**Solution**:
1. Shorten the activity; it's better to do 15 minutes of engaged play than 45 minutes of resistance
2. Change activities: Try different games; some will appeal more than others
3. Allow breaks: Frequent water, snack, and rest breaks; vary intensity
4. Simplify: Reduce complexity of tasks
5. Join in: Your participation often motivates child to continue
6. Honor preference: If child really doesn't like it, that's okay; try other outdoor activities instead

**Timeline**: Interest in survival skills grows over time for some children. Others may never develop passion (and that's fine). Variety in outdoor activities is healthy.

---

**Challenge**: Child gets frustrated when tasks are hard (knots won't tie, shelter falls down, can't find item)

**Why**: Young children have low frustration tolerance. Difficult tasks can quickly become overwhelming.

**Solution**:
1. Simplify task: Make it easier to succeed
2. Break into steps: "First this knot. Then we'll try the harder one."
3. Model process: "Watch me do it. This is how we do it."
4. Celebrate effort: "You tried hard! That's what matters."
5. Allow quitting: "We can try again tomorrow. Good effort today!"
6. Problem-solve: "It didn't work. What could we try instead?"
7. Make it a game: "Let's see how many times we can do this!"

**Timeline**: Perseverance develops gradually. Very young children will give up frequently; this is normal. Over months, they begin to persist longer.

### Progress Tracking

**Signs of skill development**:
- **Week 1-2**: Participates in activities with adult guidance; gathers materials; shows interest
- **Week 3-4**: Can gather materials more independently; beginning to understand shelter purpose
- **Month 2**: Participating in multiple skill activities; learning simple knots; showing confidence
- **Month 3**: Can gather materials efficiently; understand fire safety; beginning to build structures more independently
- **Month 4+**: Demonstrating multiple survival skills; showing problem-solving; suggesting ideas; asking to continue activities

**When to add challenge**:
- Once child comfortably participates in one skill station, add second activity
- When child shows understanding of shelter, challenge with different structure types
- When child learns basic knot, introduce new knot
- When child shows interest and confidence, introduce more realistic/challenging scenarios
- When child can follow safety rules consistently, introduce slightly more advanced skills
