# SONG: Frere Jacques (Are You Sleeping, Brother John)

Sonnez les matines, sonnez les matines,

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **SLEEPING** - Close your eyes gently while tilting your head to one side. Bring your palms together and rest them against your cheek, as if you're peacefully sleeping.

- **BROTHER** - Make a "B" handshape with your thumb extended. Place it on your cheek (male sign), then bring both hands together with fingers touching to show closeness and relation.

- **BELL** - Hold your right hand as a fist near your ear, then move it outward and slightly upward in a ringing motion, as if ringing a bell.

- **MORNING** - Hold your left arm bent across your body horizontally. Bring your right hand up from below the left arm with fingers together, moving upward to represent the sun rising.

- **DING (RING)** - Point to your ear with one hand, then make a small ringing motion near your ear with the other hand, or mime pulling a bell rope with both hands.

- **WAKE UP** - Start with hands near your face with fingers partially closed, then open your eyes wide and open your fingers as if waking from sleep.

- **QUESTION** - Bring one hand up to your chin, tilting your head slightly and raising your eyebrows to show questioning.

## Song Overview

"Frere Jacques" is a beloved international nursery rhyme that introduces young children to both French language and musical concepts like rounds and rhythm. Children are delighted by the simple repetition, the call-and-response structure, and the familiar "ding, ding, dong" sound effects that they can easily mimic. This song supports language development in multiple languages, auditory discrimination, social interaction through group singing, and gross motor skills through the accompanying motions. It's equally perfect for morning wake-up songs, transitional moments, or any time you want to engage little ones in joyful, multilingual singing.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: "Frere Jacques" is a traditional French children's song that dates back to at least the 18th century. It originated in France as a round (a song where multiple singers can begin at different times, each completing the melody while others are still singing from the beginning). The song likely has roots in monastery wake-up bell traditions, where a bell-ringer named Brother Jacques would ring the morning bells to wake the monks. It was popularized internationally in the 20th century and adapted into English as "Are You Sleeping, Brother John?" The melody is so simple and universally recognizable that it has become one of the most widely taught songs for young children learning music around the world.

**Duration**: About 1-2 minutes (depending on how many times you repeat the verse)

## Cultural Notes

This song beautifully bridges French and English-speaking cultures, making it perfect for introducing young children to foreign languages in a fun, musical context. In many bilingual households, both versions are sung interchangeably. The song's round structure has deep roots in European musical traditions and allows multiple singers of different skill levels to participate simultaneously, creating a sense of community and shared experience. Variations exist across cultures - some versions use different names or add additional verses. In Francophone countries, the song remains a staple of early childhood education, while English-speaking countries know it primarily as "Are You Sleeping, Brother John?" Teaching both versions provides children with exposure to linguistic diversity and helps caregivers share their cultural heritage with the children in their care.
