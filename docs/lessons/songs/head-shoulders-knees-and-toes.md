## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **HEAD** - Place both hands on top of your head, palms down. Tap gently on the top of your head with your fingertips a couple of times to indicate the location of the head.

- **SHOULDERS** - Point both index fingers forward, then move them backward and upward to touch both shoulders, or tap your shoulders with both hands to indicate where shoulders are located.

- **KNEES** - Bend both hands at the knuckles and tap your knees with your bent fingers to show where your knees are.

- **TOES** - Point downward with both index fingers and point to or tap your toes to show their location.

- **EYES** - Point to both eyes with your index fingers, making a small circle around each eye. You can alternate pointing to one eye, then the other.

- **EARS** - Point to both ears with your index fingers, or gently tug on your ears with both hands to indicate where ears are.

- **MOUTH** - Point to your mouth with your index finger, or draw a circle around your mouth to indicate its location.

- **NOSE** - Point to your nose with your index finger, or press your index finger against your nose to show where it is located.

## Song Overview

"Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes" is a beloved action song that makes learning body parts fun and engaging for young children. The song's simple, repetitive melody combined with physical movements helps children develop body awareness, gross motor skills, and directional language. Children love the challenge of keeping up with the increasingly faster tempos in later verses, promoting coordination and quick thinking. The concrete nature of pointing to body parts while singing supports language development and vocabulary building in a highly interactive, playful way. This song is perfect for active playtime, circle time, or anytime children need to burn energy while learning.

**Category**: Action Songs

**Origin**: While the melody is believed to have roots in earlier nursery traditions and folk music, "Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes" became widely popular in its current form during the 1960s. The song became especially well-known through children's educational media and has since become a staple in early childhood education worldwide. Various versions and adaptations exist with slightly different lyrics and body parts emphasized.

**Duration**: About 1-2 minutes (depending on tempo progression and number of repetitions)

## Cultural Notes

This song has been adapted across many cultures and languages, including Spanish (Cabeza, Hombros, Rodillas y Dedos de los Pies), French (Tête, Épaules, Genoux et Orteils), and many others. The universal appeal of learning body parts makes this song accessible and recognizable in early childhood settings worldwide. In many cultures, caregivers create variations by substituting different body parts or adding traditional movement patterns. Some versions include additional body parts like arms, hands, or chin, making the song adaptable to individual learning goals. The song's effectiveness in teaching body awareness has made it a consistent favorite across generations, remaining relevant whether sung in English or any other language.
