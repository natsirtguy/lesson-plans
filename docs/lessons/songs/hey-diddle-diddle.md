# SONG: Hey Diddle Diddle

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Hey, diddle, diddle,
The cat and the fiddle,
The cow jumped over the moon;
The little dog laughed
To see such sport,
And the dish ran away with the spoon.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **CAT** - Hold both hands up near your cheeks with fingers curled, then draw your hands back toward your temples while extending your index and middle fingers outward (like whiskers)
- **COW** - Make "Y" handshapes with both hands and place thumbs on temples, then extend hands to the sides (showing horns)
- **MOON** - Make a "C" shape with one hand at the side of your eye and look up, as if gazing at the moon in the sky
- **DOG** - Snap your fingers near your leg, then pat your leg as if calling a dog
- **LAUGHED** - Make "5" handshapes at your mouth and wiggle fingers while moving hands downward and outward (showing laughter spreading)
- **DISH** - Make a "D" handshape with one hand and trace a circle in the air to show a round dish shape
- **SPOON** - Hold one hand open facing you, then scoop upward with your other hand as if holding a spoon

## Song Overview

"Hey Diddle Diddle" is a beloved classic that captivates young children with its whimsical, nonsensical imagery and rhythmic bounce. The fantastical elements—a cat with a fiddle, a cow jumping over the moon—spark imagination and creativity in toddlers while the playful rhyme scheme supports early language development and phonemic awareness. This short, punchy song is perfect for any time of day and is especially delightful when accompanied by exaggerated facial expressions and silly movements.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: Traditional English nursery rhyme first published in 1765, though likely much older. The original author is unknown, but it's believed to have roots in English folk tradition and oral storytelling that dates back centuries.

**Duration**: About 30-45 seconds

## Cultural Notes

"Hey Diddle Diddle" is one of the most iconic nursery rhymes in the English-speaking world and has been translated and adapted in many cultures worldwide. Some scholars have attempted to find historical meanings in the rhyme (such as references to ancient events), but its enduring popularity comes from its pure nonsense and playfulness—qualities that delight toddlers regardless of cultural background. The rhyme has inspired countless adaptations, picture books, and performances, and remains a staple in early childhood settings across America, Britain, Australia, and beyond.
