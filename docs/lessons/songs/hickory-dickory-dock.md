# SONG: Hickory Dickory Dock

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Hickory dickory dock,
The mouse ran up the clock.
The clock struck one,
The mouse ran down,
Hickory dickory dock.

**Verse 2:**
Hickory dickory dock,
The mouse ran up the clock.
The clock struck two,
The mouse said "Boo!"
Hickory dickory dock.

**Verse 3:**
Hickory dickory dock,
The mouse ran up the clock.
The clock struck three,
The mouse said "Whee!"
Hickory dickory dock.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Mouse** - Hold your hand near your nose with fingers curled, then wiggle your fingers to show small whiskers twitching like a mouse
- **Ran** - Make both hands into fists and move them back and forth in quick alternating motions, moving forward to show rapid running
- **Clock** - Trace a large circle in the air in front of your face with your index finger to show the round face of a clock
- **Up** - Point your index fingers upward and move them upward to show something going up or climbing
- **Down** - Point your index fingers downward and move them downward to show something going down or climbing down
- **Struck** - Hold one hand out flat, then make a fist with the other and tap it against your open palm to show something striking or hitting
- **One** - Hold up just your index finger and tap it once to show the number one or one o'clock

## Song Overview

Hickory Dickory Dock is a delightful counting and action rhyme that captivates young children with its silly sound effects, rhythmic bouncing cadence, and engaging story about a clever mouse. The repeated refrain "Hickory dickory dock" is fun to say and sing, encouraging participation and phonemic awareness. This rhyme supports language development through enjoyable sound patterns, introduces number concepts (counting from one to three), and the up-and-down motions connect language learning to physical movement and coordination.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: Hickory Dickory Dock is believed to have originated as a children's counting-out rhyme used in games, with the nonsense phrase "Hickory dickory dock" possibly derived from old counting rhymes or nursery language. The rhyme first appeared in print in the late 18th century and has remained popular in English-speaking countries ever since. The exact origin of the "hickory dickory" phrase remains unclear, though some suggest it may relate to old children's games or mysterious rhyming traditions passed down orally for generations.

**Duration**: About 1.5-2 minutes (all three verses)

## Cultural Notes

Hickory Dickory Dock is known and loved across English-speaking countries with minor variations in different regions. The rhyme has inspired numerous extensions where additional verses are created with different numbers and different actions the mouse performs (making sounds, movements, or silly actions). In some cultures, the rhyme has been translated or adapted into different languages while trying to maintain the playful rhythm and rhyme scheme. The rhyme is frequently used in early childhood classrooms as a teaching tool for numbers, directions (up and down), and repeated patterns. Many children's books feature their own illustrated versions of this mouse and clock story.
