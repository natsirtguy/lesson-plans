# SONG: Humpty Dumpty

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Humpty Dumpty sat on a wall,
Humpty Dumpty had a great fall.
All the king's horses and all the king's men
Couldn't put Humpty together again.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Humpty/Dumpty** - Puff out your cheeks and round your arms in front of your body like an egg shape to represent the round character
- **Sat** - Place both hands palm-down in front of you, then lower them gently as if sitting down on a chair
- **Wall** - Hold both hands up in front of you, palms facing outward and slightly apart, to show a vertical wall structure
- **Fall** - Start with both hands up high, then let them drop down quickly with wiggling fingers to show something tumbling down
- **King** - Touch your right hand to your left shoulder, then to your right hip in an X pattern to show royal regalia
- **Horses** - Make a "V" shape with your first two fingers on both hands, one hand on each side of your head, and move them in a trotting motion
- **Together** - Hold both hands out to the sides with fingers extended, then bring them together in the middle to show things coming together

## Song Overview

Humpty Dumpty is a beloved nursery rhyme that tells the cautionary tale of a round egg-shaped character and his great fall. Young children are drawn to the silly character, the rhythmic bounce of the rhyme, and the dramatic action of falling. This classic supports language development through repeated rhyming sounds, builds narrative comprehension as children understand cause and effect, and encourages imaginative play about characters and consequences.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: Humpty Dumpty first appeared in print in 1810 in "Juvenile Amusements," though the rhyme likely existed orally for centuries before. **Duration**: About 30-45 seconds

## Cultural Notes

Humpty Dumpty is known across English-speaking countries with remarkably consistent lyrics, making it one of the most standardized nursery rhymes. The rhyme has become a cultural reference point, appearing in literature, films, and artwork worldwide. In some cultures, this rhyme is adapted into different languages while maintaining the rhyme scheme and character. The concept of "you can't put things back together" has made it a metaphor in adult literature and psychology. Many children's books expand on Humpty's story, creating different versions where he might be repaired or have different adventures, allowing educators to explore themes of resilience and problem-solving through this classic character.
