# SONG: Jack and Jill

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Jack and Jill went up the hill
To fetch a pail of water.
Jack fell down and broke his crown,
And Jill came tumbling after.

**Verse 2:**
Up Jack got and home did trot
As fast as he could caper,
To old Dame Dob who patched his nob
With vinegar and brown paper.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Jack** - Bring both hands up to shoulder height with palms facing each other, then tap your right shoulder twice to indicate the male character Jack
- **Jill** - Bring both hands up to shoulder height with palms facing each other, then tap your left shoulder twice to indicate the female character Jill
- **Hill** - Hold your hand out flat, then sweep it upward in a curved motion to show the shape of a hill
- **Water** - Hold your hand at mouth level with fingers relaxed, then wiggle all your fingers downward gently to represent flowing or splashing water
- **Fell** - Start with your hand up high with fingers extended, then quickly drop it down with a shaking motion to show falling
- **Ran** - Make both hands into fists and move them back and forth in quick alternating motions as if running fast
- **Crown** - Touch your two index fingers together above your head, then move them apart in an arc to show a crown shape on top of the head

## Song Overview

Jack and Jill is a classic action rhyme with a playful narrative that appeals to young children's natural curiosity about cause and effect. The repeating characters, rhythmic bouncing cadence, and dramatic action of falling make it highly engaging for ages 2-4. This rhyme supports language development through rhyming pairs, builds listening skills as children anticipate the rhyming words, and the physical movements (jumping, falling, running) connect the story to gross motor development and play.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: Jack and Jill is one of the oldest nursery rhymes in English literature, with evidence of the verse dating back to at least the 17th century. ## Cultural Notes

Jack and Jill has been adapted and referenced in countless children's books, songs, and media around the world. Different cultures and regions have created their own versions with local characters and settings, though the basic narrative of going up a hill and falling down remains recognizable. In some British versions, the second verse differs slightly, with various names used for the woman who tends to their wounds. The rhyme has inspired numerous artistic interpretations and is frequently used in children's theater and musical productions. The rhyme's themes of mishap, caring, and quick recovery help children understand narrative structure and show how characters help each other after accidents.
