# SONG: Little Jack Horner

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Little Jack Horner
Sat in the corner,
Eating his Christmas pie,
He put in his thumb,
And pulled out a plum,
And said, "What a good boy am I!"

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **LITTLE** - Hold one hand flat with fingers together in front of you, then move your other hand close to it with fingers pinched, showing something very small
- **SIT** - Hold both hands with fingers bent at the knuckles, palms down, and place one hand on top of the other in a sitting position
- **CORNER** - Point both index fingers forward, then turn them so they form a right angle, showing where two walls meet
- **EAT** - Bring your fingers and thumb together on one hand (like a pinching motion) and bring it to your mouth a few times
- **PIE** - Extend your hand flat, palm down, and cut across it with your other hand's fingers to show slicing a pie
- **THUMB** - Hold your thumbs up with both fists, or just extend one thumb upward
- **PLUM** - Form an "O" with thumb and index finger on both hands and twist them slightly to show something round and small

## Song Overview

"Little Jack Horner" is a beloved nursery rhyme that captures children's imagination with its quirky character and playful actions. The repetitive rhythm, combined with opportunities for simple gestures (sitting in a corner, pulling out a plum), makes it engaging for toddlers and preschoolers. This rhyme supports language development through rhyming patterns and can be paired with creative movement as children act out Jack's actions.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: "Little Jack Horner" first appeared in print in the 18th century as a nursery rhyme. The exact origins are unclear, though there is a legend that it may relate to a historical figure named Jack Horner who was a 16th-century steward. The rhyme has become a staple of English-language nursery rhyme collections and children's literature.

**Duration**: About 30-45 seconds

## Cultural Notes

This rhyme is well-known in English-speaking countries and has been adapted and performed in various ways for centuries. The image of Jack sitting in a corner eating pie has inspired numerous children's books, animations, and performances. In some variations, the rhyme is paired with the finger play game where children use their own thumbs to act out pulling out the plum. The mention of Christmas pie connects this rhyme to holiday traditions and gives it seasonal appeal for winter singing.
