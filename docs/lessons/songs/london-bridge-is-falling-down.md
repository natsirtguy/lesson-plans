## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Bridge** - Place one hand flat over the other at an angle, creating the shape of an arch to represent a bridge structure
- **Falling** - Hold both hands up near your shoulders with fingers pointing downward, then move them down while wiggling fingers to show something falling
- **Down** - Point your index finger downward and move it downward to emphasize direction
- **Lock** - Make both hands into fists with thumbs facing forward, then turn them as if turning a key in a lock
- **Key** - Make a fist with your index and middle fingers extended, then twist it slightly to show a key turning
- **Build** - Place one hand flat, then place the other hand on top of it, and continue stacking motions upward to show building
- **Gold** - Point to your ear with your index finger, then open both hands with fingers wiggling to show something shiny and valuable
- **Silver** - Wiggle all your fingers on both hands while moving them downward to show something sparkling and metallic

## Song Overview

"London Bridge Is Falling Down" is an action song that combines singing with a traditional bridge-building game where children form an arch and "capture" others who pass beneath it. The repetitive structure and cumulative narrative of attempting different building materials help develop language patterns, cause-and-effect understanding, and anticipation skills. The physical movement component makes it excellent for gross motor development, turn-taking, cooperative play, and building social bonds through group participation in a structured game.

**Category**: Action Songs

**Origin**: "London Bridge Is Falling Down" is one of the oldest recorded children's games, with roots possibly tracing back to the original London Bridge built by the Romans or the medieval bridge completed in 1209. The song's exact historical origins are debated among scholars, but it may reference actual bridge collapses or general bridge-building folklore. The song emerged as a children's singing game in the 18th century and spread throughout English-speaking countries where it remains beloved today.

**Duration**: About 2-3 minutes (depending on verses performed)

## Cultural Notes

This classic game is played in many countries across Europe and beyond, each with delightful local variations and translations. In France it's called "Pont d'Avignon," in Germany "Brückenbau," in Sweden "Bron är bruten," and in the Netherlands "Brug, brug, houdt stand." Some versions have different lyrics describing various fates for those caught in the arch, such as being locked up, becoming guards, or having to make a choice. The song's themes of building, collapse, and rebuilding appear in nursery rhymes across cultures, suggesting that bridge games and songs may be universal human creations that help children understand structural concepts through play.
