# SONG: Oh Where Oh Where Has My Little Dog Gone

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Oh where, oh where has my little dog gone?
Oh where, oh where can he be?
With his ears cut short and his tail cut long,
Oh where, oh where can he be?

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **WHERE** - Hold both hands up with palms facing outward and shoulders shrugged slightly, then move both hands side to side as if searching or questioning
- **DOG** - Pat your leg a couple of times with your fingers to call a dog, then bring your hand up to your ear and snap your fingers (representing the ears)
- **EARS** - Point to one ear with both index fingers, then move them slightly forward and back to show the shape of ears
- **TAIL** - Make a curved shape with one arm behind your back and move it side to side to represent a wagging tail
- **LONG** - Hold both hands up with index fingers and thumbs extended, then move them upward to show length
- **SHORT** - Hold both hands at the same height in front of you and tap your index fingers together, showing a short distance
- **LOST/GONE** - Start with both hands together in front of your chest and open them outward and upward as if something is disappearing

## Song Overview

This cheerful nursery rhyme combines the mystery and emotion of a "lost pet" with playful repetition that young children love to mimic. The repeated "oh where" refrain helps develop listening and memory skills, while the simple narrative about a little dog makes it relatable to children who have pets or know about dogs. The song works wonderfully as a search or hiding game, encouraging physical movement and interactive play, and it's gentle enough for calmer moments while still holding children's attention through the engaging story.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: A traditional English nursery rhyme with roots dating back to at least the 19th century. The exact composer is unknown, but the song became popular as a children's playground chant and singing game in English-speaking countries. It has been passed down through generations as part of oral children's literature.

**Duration**: About 45 seconds to 1 minute
