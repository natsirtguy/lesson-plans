## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **FARM** - Hold both hands at chest level with fingers spread, palms facing out. Cross arms horizontally across the body, then move them outward on both sides as if pushing open barn doors.

- **ANIMAL** - Place both hands on shoulders with fingers down and arms crossed. Gently shake both hands forward and backward, wiggling fingers to show fur or texture of animals.

- **COW** - Make the sign for "horns" by extending both index fingers and thumbs from each hand near temples, pointing upward and outward. Then move hands down to milk the cow by making a squeezing motion with hands at chest level.

- **PIG** - Hook your index finger and pinky finger on one hand, placing them at the tip of your nose pointing downward, then twist hand back to represent a pig's snout.

- **SHEEP** - Place your hand on your arm and make a zigzag motion upward from wrist to shoulder with your other hand's fingers to show wool.

- **DUCK** - Bring all four fingers and thumb together at your mouth to form a beak shape, opening and closing them while moving your hand forward (like a duck's bill moving through water).

- **SOUND/NOISE** - Hold both index fingers at ear level pointing backward. Move them down and forward in a wavy motion to show sound waves traveling.

- **FARM-ANIMALS** - Start with the FARM sign, then point around as if showing different animals scattered throughout the property.

## Song Overview

"Old MacDonald Had a Farm" is one of the most beloved children's songs, capturing young toddlers' fascination with animals and their sounds. The repetitive structure and predictable pattern help children anticipate and participate, while the opportunity to make animal sounds during each verse delights children while supporting language development and phonemic awareness. This song builds confidence as children learn they can "sing" the animal sounds themselves, encouraging vocal exploration and turn-taking in musical engagement.

**Category**: Nature & Animal Songs

**Origin**: This American folk song has roots in 19th-century agricultural traditions. The earliest known printed version appeared in the early 1900s. The song's simple, repeatable structure made it perfect for entertainment during farm chores and long journeys. While the original songwriter is unknown, the song was popularized through oral tradition and became a staple of children's education by the mid-20th century.

**Duration**: About 2-3 minutes (depending on how many animal verses you include)

## Cultural Notes
