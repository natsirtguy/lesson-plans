# SONG: Peter Peter Pumpkin Eater

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Peter, Peter pumpkin eater
Had a wife and couldn't keep her
Put her in a pumpkin shell
And there he kept her very well

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Peter** - Point to yourself or an imaginary person, then spell out P-E-T-E-R using fingerspelling, or use the name sign if established
- **Pumpkin** - Make a fist with both hands at chest level, then tap the knuckles together a couple of times to show a round shape
- **Wife** - Touch your thumb to your chin (female sign) with thumb extended, then clasp both hands together in front of chest (indicating a bond/relationship)
- **Shell** - Curve both hands like a dome or shell shape and place over the top of your head or body to show something covering
- **Keep** - Hold both hands in front of you with palms facing down and move them toward yourself with a gentle pulling motion, repeat

## Song Overview

"Peter Peter Pumpkin Eater" is a delightful nursery rhyme that captivates toddlers with its silly storyline and fun-to-say words. The repetition of "pumpkin" and the imaginative image of someone living inside a pumpkin shell spark joy and giggles in young children. This song supports language development, rhythm awareness, and memory skills through its predictable structure and rhyming pattern. It's perfect for any time of day, especially in the fall season.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: This is a traditional English nursery rhyme with roots dating back to the 1600s-1700s. The original verse likely had romantic or humorous origins in folk tradition, though the exact authorship is unknown. The rhyme has been passed down through generations and appears in various children's literature collections dating back centuries.

**Duration**: About 30-45 seconds

## Cultural Notes

"Peter Peter Pumpkin Eater" is known worldwide in English-speaking countries and appears in numerous international children's books and collections. The rhyme has inspired creative adaptations, including new verses and variations in different regions. In some versions, caregivers substitute the child's name for additional personalization. The pumpkin imagery connects naturally to harvest celebrations and fall traditions, making it especially relevant during autumn months. Different cultures have created their own rhythmic chants and stories with similar patterns, demonstrating the universal appeal of repetitive, silly storytelling for young children.
