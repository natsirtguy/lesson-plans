# SONG: Polly Put the Kettle On

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Polly put the kettle on,
Polly put the kettle on,
Polly put the kettle on,
We'll all have tea.

**Verse 2:**
Sukey take it off again,
Sukey take it off again,
Sukey take it off again,
They've all gone away.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **POLLY** - Make the "P" handshape and place it on your shoulder (representing a proper name or person's name sign)
- **KETTLE** - Make a circular shape with both hands to show a round kettle, then pretend to hold a handle and pour
- **PUT** - Hold both hands open with palms up, then move them downward to show placing something down
- **TEA** - Pinch your thumb and index finger together, pretend to hold a tea bag, then dip it into an imaginary cup
- **SUKEY** - Make the "S" handshape and place it on your shoulder (representing another person's name)
- **TAKE** - Extend both hands out with fingers spread, then curl them inward toward your body as if taking something
- **OFF** - Place your hand on top of your other hand, then brush it off and away to show removal
- **AWAY** - Hold both hands up in front of you, then move them backward and outward in a sweeping motion

## Song Overview

"Polly Put the Kettle On" is a delightful action and role-play song that introduces young children to simple domestic routines and tea-time traditions in a fun, musical way. The repetition of the verse lines helps with memory development and language learning, while the clear action sequence (putting the kettle on, then taking it off) supports children's understanding of cause-and-effect and sequential thinking. This song works beautifully as both an interactive game during snack time or tea time and as a pretend-play activity, encouraging imaginative play and social interaction among young children.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: A traditional English nursery rhyme and singing game with roots in 18th-century children's culture. The rhyme was often used as a counting-out game or action game in playgrounds and schoolyards. It reflects British cultural customs around tea time and domestic routines, and likely originated in England before spreading to other English-speaking countries.

**Duration**: About 1-1.5 minutes

## Cultural Notes

This traditional English children's song celebrates the cultural significance of tea time in British culture, making it a gentle introduction to cultural practices for young children. In the UK and Commonwealth countries, tea time remains an important daily ritual, and this song has been used for generations to teach children about these routines. The song often appears in collections of traditional British nursery rhymes and has inspired variations in different countries. In some regions, the song is used as an interactive game where children take turns playing the roles of "Polly" and "Sukey," fostering collaborative play and turn-taking skills. The simple domestic scenario makes it relatable across cultures, even for children unfamiliar with formal tea time traditions.
