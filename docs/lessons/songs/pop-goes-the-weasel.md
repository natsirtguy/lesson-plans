# SONG: Pop Goes the Weasel

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
All around the mulberry bush
The monkey chased the weasel
The monkey thought 'twas all in fun
Pop! goes the weasel

**Verse 2:**
A penny for a spool of thread
A penny for a needle
That's the way the money goes
Pop! goes the weasel

**Verse 3:**
Half a pound of tuppenny rice
Half a pound of treacle
Mix it all up nice and tight
Pop! goes the weasel

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **MONKEY** - Place your hands under your armpits and make scratching motions while making a monkey face or expression
- **WEASEL** - Use one hand with fingers moving in a sinuous, slithering motion along your arm to show how the weasel moves
- **CHASE** - Make one hand into a fist and move it behind your other hand which is also in a fist, showing one following the other in motion
- **POP** - Hold both hands together in front of you, then quickly pull them apart while making a popping motion with your mouth
- **MULBERRY** - Pretend to pick berries by making pinching motions with your fingers and moving your hand as if reaching into branches
- **MONEY** - Rub your thumb against your first two fingers together, the universal gesture for money
- **BUSH** - Hold both hands up with fingers spread wide and slightly curved to form the shape of a round bush
- **SPOOL** - Create a circular shape with both hands to show the round spool of thread

## Song Overview

"Pop Goes the Weasel" is a beloved nursery rhyme that delights young children with its playful rhythm, silly imagery, and the exciting surprise of the "pop" at the end of each verse. The unpredictable punchline helps develop auditory anticipation and encourages active listening. This song supports language development through repetition, introduces children to market/old-fashioned vocabulary in a fun context, and the physical "pop" movement helps develop gross motor coordination and timing awareness.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

## Cultural Notes

"Pop Goes the Weasel" has been passed down through generations and appears in cultures across the English-speaking world, with similar versions and melodies known in Great Britain, the United States, Canada, and Australia. The song references old-fashioned currency (pennies and tuppence) and marketplace items (thread, needles, rice, treacle) that connect children to historical commerce and daily life from centuries past. In some regions, the song is paired with a playground game where children dance in a circle and someone pops out or chases others around the "mulberry bush." Various adaptations exist worldwide, with different lyrics but similar playful "pop" surprise elements, showing how children's songs evolve and adapt across cultures while maintaining their core appeal.
