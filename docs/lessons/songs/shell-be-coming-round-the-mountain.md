## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **MOUNTAIN** - Make both hands into fists with thumbs up, then move them upward in a peak shape to show a mountain's triangular silhouette.
- **COME** - Crook your index fingers and move both hands toward your body in a "come here" motion, or use one hand to beckon.
- **HORSES** - Make both hands into fists, place them near the sides of your head like horse ears, then move them in a trotting motion.
- **DRIVING** - Make both hands into fists as if holding a steering wheel, then turn them back and forth in steering motions.
- **MEET** - Hold both hands in front of you with palms facing inward, then bring them together until the fingertips touch.
- **FOOD** - Put all fingers and thumb together (like a pinched hand), place near your mouth, then move downward to show food going down.
- **HAPPY** - Place both hands on your chest with palms inward, then move them upward and outward with a smile to show happiness and celebration.
- **FAMILY** - Make an "F" shape with both hands, then move them in a circular motion around your body to show togetherness and family unity.

## Song Overview

She'll Be Coming Round the Mountain is an energetic, call-and-response style song that begs for movement, clapping, and silly sound effects that young children adore. The repetitive structure with its "when she comes" refrain makes it easy for toddlers to anticipate and join in, supporting language development and rhythmic awareness. The vivid imagery of horses, mountains, and gathering for a celebration sparks imagination while the song's upbeat tempo and opportunities for action movements (tooting horns, cracking whips, clapping) make it ideal for active play, developing gross motor skills, and building joyful connections with others.

**Category**: Action Songs

**Origin**: This American folk song emerged in the 19th century, likely as an adaptation of earlier British folk melodies. Some scholars believe it may have spiritual or railroad worker song origins, as it contains elements common to both traditions. The song became especially popular in the American South and Appalachian regions, and it spread widely through folk music traditions. Variations exist across different regions, with some versions emphasizing different modes of transportation (trains instead of horses) or different foods. The exact original composer is unknown, but the song has remained beloved in American folk culture for over 150 years.

**Duration**: About 1-2 minutes (depending on how many verses are sung)

## Cultural Notes

This song exists in many regional variations, with different communities substituting local foods, animals, or details that reflect their cultures. In some versions, "She" becomes "He," and other verses mention trains instead of horses, reflecting the song's evolution as it traveled across America. The song's participatory nature—with opportunities for children to add sound effects like "Yay!" at the end of choruses or create their own movement interpretations—makes it beloved in classrooms and at gatherings. In some cultures, similar call-and-response songs serve similar functions of building anticipation and community participation. The song teaches children about hospitality, community gathering, and celebration while honoring American folk traditions.
