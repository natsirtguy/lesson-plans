## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **SKIP** - Make a skipping motion with both hands together, alternating up and down like skipping rope or hopping
- **PARTNER** - Point two index fingers toward each other as if they are in partnership or unity
- **LOST** - Hold both hands up in "Y" position and shake them slightly, expressing uncertainty or not knowing where something is
- **BETTER** - Point to one side with flat hand, then sweep hand upward and to the other side to show comparison
- **FLIES** - Wiggle fingers downward in a fluttering motion to show flying insects
- **SHOO** - Push hand forward with a waving motion as if chasing something away
- **BUTTERMILK** - Make circular motions with both hands in front of body as if churning or stirring
- **DARLING** - Cross both arms over heart or hug yourself to show affection and endearment

## Song Overview

"Skip to My Lou" is a cheerful American folk song that combines active movement with playful humor and delightful nonsense verses. This song energizes young children with its bouncy rhythm and inviting refrain while supporting motor development through skipping and dancing movements. The repetitive structure and silly imagery (flies in buttermilk, hair in cream jars) capture toddlers' imagination and humor, developing rhythm awareness, language skills, and pure joyful engagementâ€”making it perfect for energetic playtime or as a transition song between activities.

**Category**: Folk & Traditional

**Origin**: American folk song that emerged in the 19th century, with roots in earlier folk traditions. "Lou" may have originally referred to "Lou-ey" (a type of American pioneer game) or could be a corruption of "loop" or "low." The song became widely known through American folk tradition and appears in early children's songbooks. It has maintained popularity through classroom usage and folk music revival movements of the 20th century.

**Duration**: About 2-3 minutes

## Cultural Notes

This song is distinctly American in its origins and style, reflecting 19th-century rural American life with references to farm activities and domestic challenges. Regional variations exist with different verses substituting local animals or situations (some versions mention a dog in the kitchen or other creatures). The song has been adapted internationally but retains its strongest connection to American folk tradition. Teachers and families often create their own silly verses, maintaining the song as a living tradition where each generation adds their own humor and imagery. The playful nature and adaptability of "Skip to My Lou" make it a wonderful tool for teaching children about cultural heritage while encouraging creativity and spontaneous fun.
