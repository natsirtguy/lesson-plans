# SONG: Star Light Star Bright

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Star light, star bright,
First star I see tonight,
I wish I may, I wish I might,
Have the wish I wish tonight.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Star** - Hold up both hands with fingers spread in a V-shape. Alternately move them upward while opening and closing fingers, creating a twinkling effect.
- **Light** - Hold both hands together near one cheek, then open them outward with fingers spreading to show light radiating out.
- **Bright** - Start with hands near face, then move them outward and upward while opening all fingers to show brightness spreading.
- **Wish** - Draw one hand down across your chest from shoulder to hip, then place both hands together in front of your chest as if holding a wish.
- **Night** - Hold one arm horizontal at shoulder height, then bring the other hand down across it, showing darkness covering the day.
- **See** - Make a V-shape with your index and middle fingers and point them at your eyes, then away from your face to show looking.
- **May** - Hold both hands in front with palms up and move them slightly upward in a questioning or hopeful gesture.

## Song Overview

Star Light, Star Bright is a timeless wishing rhyme that combines the magic of stargazing with the comfort of nighttime routines for young children. This gentle, contemplative song teaches children about making wishes, encourages them to observe nature, and promotes calm, reflective thinking at bedtime. The simple, repetitive lyrics make it perfect for 2-4 year olds to memorize and learn, while the short length means it can be sung as part of an evening wind-down ritual without overstimulating tired children.

**Category**: Lullabies

**Origin**: This is a traditional English nursery rhyme with origins traced back to the 18th century; the exact author is unknown, but it has been passed down through generations. Some variations of the rhyme appear in earlier folk traditions, and the modern version became standardized in the early 19th century. It reflects a time when children truly depended on starlight to mark the coming of night.

**Duration**: About 30 seconds to 1 minute

## Cultural Notes

Star Light, Star Bright is recognized across English-speaking cultures and has been adapted into various forms in different countries. Many cultures have similar wishing-upon-a-star traditions, though this particular rhyme is most common in American and British early childhood education. The practice of making a wish on the first star reflects ancient stargazing customs and is now a beloved part of bedtime routines in many households. Caregivers often use this rhyme to help children transition to sleep while fostering a sense of wonder about the natural world and the magic of nighttime.
