# SONG: Take Me Out to the Ball Game

## Lyrics

**Verse 1:**
Take me out to the ball game,
Take me out to the crowd,
Buy me some peanuts and Cracker Jack,
I don't care if I never get back.
Let me root, root, root for the home team,
If they don't win, it's a shame.
For it's one, two, three strikes, you're out,
At the old ball game.

## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **BALL** - Form both hands into curved C-shapes facing each other at chest level. Keep them about 6 inches apart to show the size and shape of a baseball.

- **GAME** - Hold both hands in front of you with index and middle fingers extended (like a "V" shape). Bounce both hands together in an alternating up-and-down motion to show playful competition.

- **ROOT** - Place both hands near your mouth with index fingers pointing upward. Push both hands forward and downward in an emphatic cheering motion, as if shouting encouragement.

- **HOME** - Touch the fingertips of both hands to your cheeks. Bring your hands down and together in front of you, angling them to form a roof shape above your head.

- **OUT** - Make an open hand shape with palm facing down. Sweep your hand outward and away from your body in a quick, decisive motion.

- **CROWD** - Make both hands into "C" shapes with palms facing each other. Place them in front of you and expand them outward to the sides to show a large group of people.

- **WIN** - Hold both hands in front of you with index and middle fingers extended in a "V" shape. Move both hands upward toward your face in a celebratory pumping motion.

- **STRIKE** - Make a fist with your dominant hand. Move it forward in a sharp, emphatic motion as if calling out "strike" with authority.

## Song Overview

"Take Me Out to the Ball Game" is an energetic and celebratory song that captures the joy and excitement of attending a baseball game. Young children love the infectious rhythm, the enthusiastic "root, root, root" chant, and the opportunity to jump, clap, and move along with the music. This classic American song introduces children to baseball culture, teaches rhythm and rhyme, builds early number recognition through the "one, two, three strikes" counting, and provides wonderful opportunities for gross motor movements and group participation. The song's upbeat tempo, clear lyrics, and playful mentions of ballpark treats make it perfect for active play time, transitions between activities, or anytime you want to energize a group of young learners.

**Category**: Folk & Traditional

**Origin**: Written in 1908 by Jack Norworth (lyrics) and Albert Von Tilzer (music), this iconic American song is one of the oldest and most recognizable tunes in American culture. Interestingly, neither composer had attended a baseball game when they wrote the songâ€”yet they captured the spirit of American baseball culture perfectly! The song premiered in a Broadway musical and has since become the unofficial anthem of baseball, traditionally performed during the seventh-inning stretch at professional games for over a century. It remains one of the few songs from that era that children and adults worldwide still recognize and sing together.

**Duration**: About 2-3 minutes (depending on how many verses you sing and your tempo)

## Cultural Notes

"Take Me Out to the Ball Game" is deeply woven into American culture and represents one of the oldest, most recognizable pieces of Americana. It is traditionally sung at professional and amateur baseball games across North America, particularly during the seventh-inning stretch, making it a participatory experience for millions of fans annually. While primarily rooted in American baseball tradition, the song has become recognized internationally as an iconic American tune. Many countries have adapted versions of this song with their own local sports (cricket, football/soccer, Australian Rules football, etc.) and local snack references. Teaching this song to young children helps them connect to American cultural heritage, introduces them to one of the nation's favorite pastimes, and prepares them to feel part of a larger community tradition should they ever attend a live baseball game. The song's spirit of community enjoyment, celebration, and simple pleasures translates across cultures worldwide.
