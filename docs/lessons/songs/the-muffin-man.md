## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **Muffin** - Hold your hands in a "C" shape in front of your chest, palms facing each other, to show the size and shape of a muffin
- **Know** - Bring your open hand up to your forehead with your palm facing inward, then move it down slightly to show knowledge in your mind
- **Man** - Start with your open hand on your chin or chest, then open both hands in front of your body to show a man's shape
- **Lives** - Hold both open hands with palms facing up on either side of your body and move them upward to show life and living
- **Nice/Nicest** - Brush your open hand down the middle of your chest with a smooth motion to show something pleasant or nice
- **Makes/Make** - Hold both hands open in front of your body and bring them together in a motion showing the creation or making of something
- **Lane** - Hold both hands flat with palms facing each other and move them forward together to show a path or lane
- **Do** - Hold both hands in loose fists and move them back and forth slightly to show action or doing something

## Song Overview

"The Muffin Man" is a traditional nursery rhyme with a catchy, memorable melody that helps children build memory skills through repetition and predictable phrasing. The song introduces young children to community helpers and trades through the character of the muffin man, while its simple structure supports language development and call-and-response interactions between caregiver and child. The repetitive verse structure makes it ideal for toddlers learning to anticipate patterns and participate in singing.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: Based on a historical figure, Thomas Farriner became known as "the muffin man" after he was a notable baker and street vendor on Drury Lane in London during the 17th century. The nursery rhyme version became popular in the 19th century and has remained a beloved classic in English-speaking nurseries ever since.

**Duration**: About 1-2 minutes

## Cultural Notes

"The Muffin Man" has deep British roots, originating from London's West End, where Drury Lane remains a famous street known for its theatrical history. The song reflects the historical reality of street vendors who sold fresh baked goods to communities, a tradition that continues in many cultures. Different countries have created similar songs featuring their own beloved local bakers and vendors—from the ice cream man to bread sellers—making this song an excellent springboard for discussing cultural professions and community traditions with young children.
