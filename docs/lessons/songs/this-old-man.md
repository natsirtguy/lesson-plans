## ASL Signs

Learn these signs to use while singing:

- **OLD** - Move your hand up from your chin to your cheek, making a fist and releasing it as if showing age lines on your face.
- **MAN** - Point to your forehead, then move both hands down the sides of your body (showing the outline of a man).
- **PLAY** - Make both hands into fists with thumbs and index fingers extended, and twist them back and forth in a playful motion.
- **ONE, TWO, THREE** - Hold up the corresponding number of fingers on one hand in a clear counting motion.
- **DOG** - Snap your fingers or pat your leg with one hand, then make a panting dog mouth motion with your other hand.
- **BONE** - Make two fists facing each other with thumbs up, then rotate them away from each other to show the shape of a bone.
- **KNICK-KNACK** - Tap your thumb with your index and middle fingers repeatedly, making a playful tapping motion.

## Song Overview

This counting and rhyming song captivates young children with its silly rhymes, rhythmic "knick-knack, paddy-whack" refrain, and the exciting opportunity to touch different body parts and objects. The numbered verses (one through ten) support early math concepts and number recognition while the bouncy melody keeps children engaged and entertained. The nonsensical nature of pairing numbers with random body parts and objects encourages imagination and humor, key developmental needs for toddlers, while the repetitive structure makes it easy to remember and predict upcoming verses.

**Category**: Nursery Rhymes

**Origin**: This traditional children's song has roots in old playground games and possibly folk origins dating back centuries. The melody and lyrics evolved over time and became standardized in the 19th and 20th centuries, appearing in numerous nursery rhyme collections. The "knick-knack, paddy-whack" refrain appears to be a traditional nonsense phrase from British children's games.

**Duration**: About 4-5 minutes (singing through all ten verses)

## Cultural Notes

"This Old Man" is recognized across English-speaking countries and has been adapted in many cultures with slightly different lyrics and activities. The song serves as an excellent tool for teaching numbers one through ten in a memorable, musical way. Some cultural variations include different objects or body parts based on regional traditions and available items. The song's simple structure makes it easy for caregivers to create additional verses with new rhyming words and actions, allowing families to personalize it with culturally relevant or family-specific references.
