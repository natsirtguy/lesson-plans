# 📚 KNOWLEDGE, SKILLS & CULTURE: Hotel Management

## 🎯 Activity Summary

Children turn the living room into a working hotel, playing every role: the guest who checks in, the receptionist who hands over a key, the housekeeper who restocks fresh towels, and the manager who solves problems. Through layered pretend play, children encounter real concepts like *reservations*, *amenities*, *check-out*, and *hospitality*, and learn that running a hotel is a coordinated dance of people taking care of strangers as if they were honored visitors.

## Vocabulary

**Reservation**: A promise to save something for someone. When a family reserves a hotel room, the hotel marks that room as theirs for specific dates so no one else can take it. People also reserve tables at restaurants and seats on airplanes — it's a way to plan ahead.

**Reception (front desk)**: The counter near a hotel's front door where guests check in. The person who works there — the *receptionist* — greets every guest, finds their reservation, and hands them their key. Reception comes from the word *receive*, because that's where guests are received into the hotel.

**Concierge**: A specially trained hotel worker (pronounced "con-see-AIRZH") whose whole job is helping guests with whatever they need: directions, restaurant suggestions, taxis, tickets, lost umbrellas. The word comes from French and means *keeper of the candles* — a very old job from when guests needed help finding their rooms at night.

**Hospitality**: The art of taking care of guests and making them feel welcome. Hotels are part of the *hospitality industry* — meaning their whole business is hospitality. The word shares its root with *hospital* and *host* — all about caring for visitors.

**Amenities**: Extra things a hotel gives guests beyond just a bed. Tiny shampoo bottles, soft slippers, a chocolate on the pillow, a swimming pool, breakfast — these are all amenities. The fancier the hotel, the fancier the amenities.

**Check-in and Check-out**: The two ceremonies every guest performs. *Check-in* is when you arrive: you tell the receptionist your name, you get your key, your stay begins. *Check-out* is when you leave: you return the key, the hotel finds out if you used anything from the minibar, and your stay ends. Hotels track which rooms are *occupied* (someone is in them), *vacant* (empty), or *being cleaned*.

**Occupancy**: How full the hotel is. If a hotel has 100 rooms and 80 are filled with guests, its occupancy is 80%. Managers watch occupancy carefully — empty rooms earn no money, but a too-full hotel might mean turning guests away.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

The home becomes a hotel for the afternoon. The child plays each role in turn — guest, receptionist, housekeeper, and manager — using real hotel scripts and real hotel objects. The play teaches role-switching, sequence-following, customer service language, and the idea that a business is many people coordinating.

**Activity Type**: Role-play / Imaginative simulation / Social-emotional learning

**Duration**: 20-35 minutes

**Setup time**: 10-15 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- A small table or counter (the "front desk")
- A bell (a real one or a toy — the classic hotel desk bell)
- Paper and crayons to make signs ("Welcome," "Room 1," "Room 2," "Pool")
- Old keys, plastic keycards, or laminated paper "key cards"
- A bedroom or hallway designated as "Room 1" (and "Room 2" if siblings or stuffed animals will be guests)
- A small suitcase or backpack (the guest's luggage)
- Folded washcloths or hand towels (the "fresh towels" for housekeeping)

**Optional**:
- A toy phone or old phone for taking reservations
- A small notebook (the "guest registry")
- Travel-size shampoo and soap bottles (amenities!)
- Hotel-themed dress-up: a bow tie or vest for the receptionist, an apron for housekeeping
- A pretend chocolate or mint to leave on the pillow
- A "minibar" — a small basket with snack-sized treats
- Tiny "Do Not Disturb" door hanger (make one with paper and string)

**Cost**: Free with household items; $5-10 for fancy add-ons like dress-up vests

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Set up the front desk with the bell, a notebook, and paper key cards
2. Make signs for each "room" and tape them on doors
3. Pack a small suitcase with stuffed animals or a change of clothes
4. Fold a stack of clean towels for housekeeping rounds
5. Pre-draw a simple grid of rooms in the notebook for tracking occupancy (you'll demonstrate this during play)

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (3-5 min):
   - Sit with the child and ask: "Have you ever stayed in a hotel? What was it like?"
   - Explain: "A hotel is a building where families pay to sleep when they're away from home. Hotels have lots of rooms — sometimes hundreds! — and there are people working there whose job is to take care of every guest. That's called *hospitality*."
   - Show the materials: "Today we're going to BUILD a hotel and run it together. I'll be the manager first, and you can be a guest. Then we'll switch."
   - Walk around and label the spaces: "This couch is the lobby. This table is *reception*. Down the hall is *Room 1*."

2. **Round 1 — The Child as Guest** (5-7 min):
   - Adult plays receptionist. Stand behind the desk.
   - Child walks in (with suitcase, holding a stuffed animal): "Welcome to our hotel! Do you have a *reservation*?"
   - Pretend to check the notebook: "Yes! I see your name right here. You're in Room 1. Here is your key. *Check-out* is at 11 in the morning. The pool is down the hall. Would you like a chocolate?"
   - Walk the child to their room. Show them the towels, the bed, the (pretend) minibar
   - Ring the bell back at the desk: "If you need anything, come ring the bell or call from your room phone. That's called calling the *concierge*."

3. **Round 2 — Housekeeping Round** (5-7 min):
   - Switch roles: the child is now housekeeping, you are the guest
   - Hand them the stack of folded towels
   - Knock on the door of "Room 1": "Housekeeping! May I come in?"
   - Model the script: "First we knock and wait. We never go in without permission, even if we work here. That's *hospitality* — treating guests' rooms like their home."
   - Walk through the room with the child: stack fresh towels, fluff the pillow, place a "chocolate" (or sticker) on the pillow, empty the (pretend) trash
   - Discuss: "Housekeepers clean a LOT of rooms every day — sometimes 15 or more. They have to work fast but also be very gentle with guests' things."

4. **Round 3 — Problem-Solving as Manager** (5-7 min):
   - Switch again: the child is now the *manager*. You are a guest with a problem.
   - Approach the front desk with an issue: "Excuse me — there are no more towels in my room! What can you do?"
   - Coach the child through the manager's response: "A good manager listens, says 'I'm so sorry,' and finds a solution. They might call housekeeping, or send fresh towels right away."
   - Run through 2-3 problems: the room is too cold, the TV won't turn on, I lost my key, my neighbor is too loud
   - Discuss: "Sometimes a manager can't fix the problem perfectly. Then they offer something to make it better — maybe a free breakfast, or moving you to a different room. That's called *making it right*."

5. **Wrap-Up: Check-Out Ceremony** (3-5 min):
   - End with a formal check-out: child plays receptionist again
   - Adult-guest returns the key: "We had a wonderful stay. Thank you!"
   - Receptionist: "Did you use anything from the minibar?"
   - Add up a pretend bill on paper: "Your room was 100 dollars, plus 5 dollars for a soda from the minibar. That's 105 dollars."
   - Hand over the bill, accept "payment" (a fake card or paper money)
   - Receptionist: "Thank you for staying with us! Please come back!"
   - Recap with the child: "There are SO many jobs in a hotel: receptionist, housekeeping, concierge, manager, cook, gardener, lifeguard. They all work together so guests feel taken care of."

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
- Skip the manager role — focus on guest and housekeeping
- Use shorter scripts: "Hi! Welcome! Here's your key!"
- Use stuffed animals as guests so the child can be receptionist while you guide
- Emphasize sensory parts: pressing the bell, fluffing pillows, hanging the "Do Not Disturb" sign
- Total time: 15-20 minutes maximum
- Don't worry about money or check-out — just play arrival and housekeeping

**3-4+ years**:
- Introduce real vocabulary: *reservation, concierge, occupancy, amenities, hospitality*
- Have the child write (or scribble) names in the guest registry
- Calculate simple bills: "Your room was 2 nights, that's 2 chocolates owed"
- Introduce more roles: the chef in the hotel restaurant, the bellhop who carries luggage, the lifeguard at the pool
- Pretend to take a reservation over the phone ("For how many nights? How many guests?")
- Discuss different kinds of hotels: a tiny inn, a giant resort, a bed-and-breakfast in someone's home

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1: Hotel Restaurant**
Add a restaurant role. The child takes orders from "guests" on a small notepad, "cooks" food in the play kitchen, and delivers room service on a tray. Practices writing, sequence, and customer service language.

**Variation 2: Big Hotel, Many Rooms**
Use the whole house as a hotel. Each bedroom is a room, the bathroom is the pool, the kitchen is the restaurant. Track *occupancy*: which rooms are full, which are empty, which are being cleaned. Use a small whiteboard or paper grid as a "front desk display."

**Variation 3: Hotels Around the World**
Look at pictures online of hotels in different places: an ice hotel in Sweden, an underwater hotel in the Maldives, a treehouse hotel in the rainforest, a tent in a desert. Discuss what makes each special. Have the child design their own hotel on paper: where would it be? What would its amenities be?

**Variation 4: Difficult Guest Scenarios**
Pretend to be a guest with increasingly tricky problems: a lost passport, a sick child, a fear of heights and assigned to the top floor. Have the child-manager problem-solve. Builds empathy and creative thinking.

**Repeat Play**: Real hotel stays become rich opportunities — point out reception, ring the bell with permission, notice the housekeeping cart, ask the concierge a real question. Each return to this game gets richer with real-world references.

## 📝 Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: My child wants to be the guest every time and doesn't want to switch roles

**Solution**: This is normal — the guest role is the most fun. Try framing the switch as "Now you get to be the BOSS of the whole hotel" (manager) or "You get to use the special towels" (housekeeping). You can also use a timer: "We're guests for 5 minutes, then we switch." Or invite a sibling or stuffed animal to be a co-guest so the child can stay in the role part of the time.

**Challenge**: The play falls apart when there's nothing for the "manager" to do

**Solution**: Bring on the problems! A hotel without complaints isn't realistic anyway. Pretend to be a guest who needs ice, who locked themselves out, whose neighbor is snoring. Each problem gives the manager something to solve. Keep a list of 10 standard hotel problems on a sticky note for quick reference.

**Challenge**: My child gets stuck on one detail (the bell, the key card)

**Solution**: Let them. Repeated ringing of the bell IS the experience. The play is for them, not for completionism. If they want to ring the bell 50 times, that's 50 reps of cause-and-effect and 50 chances to greet a "guest." Eventually they'll want to move on.

**Challenge**: My child invents money rules that don't make sense

**Solution**: This is fine! Pretend economics is part of the fun. If a "guest" needs to pay 1,000 dollars for a banana, go with it. Don't correct — just play. You can layer in real concepts later by example: "I'll pay 5 dollars for this banana. Is that enough?"

### Follow-Up Activities

1. **Quick reinforcement**: Next time you walk by a hotel, point it out. "Look — that's a hotel! See the people pulling suitcases? They're checking in."

2. **Real hotel observation**: Next time you stay at a hotel, give the child small "missions": find the bell, count the elevators, count how many towels are in the bathroom, find the housekeeping cart. Each observation reinforces the play.

3. **Build it bigger**: Use cardboard boxes to build a tiny model hotel with multiple floors. Label each room. Use small figurines as guests. Play "managing the hotel" in miniature.

4. **Reading connection**: Books like *Eloise* (the Plaza Hotel), *Madeline's Rescue* (Paris hotel), or any picture book with a hotel setting reinforce the vocabulary. Read together and point out reception, the concierge, the doorman.

5. **Service appreciation**: Next time someone helps your family in a service job (a waiter, a store clerk, a hotel worker), make a point of thanking them by their role. "Thank you for taking care of us today" reinforces hospitality as something to notice and appreciate.
