# 📚 KNOWLEDGE, SKILLS & CULTURE: How Speakers Work

## 🎯 Activity Summary

Children discover the surprising secret hidden inside every speaker: a tiny magnet and a coil of wire that wiggle a paper cone back and forth thousands of times per second to push waves of air at our ears. Through touching a playing speaker to feel the vibrations, building a simple "cup-string telephone," and watching salt or rice dance on a speaker, children build intuition for sound as moving air — and for how electricity, magnets, and motion combine to make music.

## Vocabulary

**Vibration**: A very fast back-and-forth wiggle. When something vibrates, it moves toward you and away from you over and over so quickly that it can be hard to see — but you can usually feel or hear it. Speakers work because a part inside them vibrates.

**Sound wave**: A pattern of squishes and stretches that travels through the air. When a speaker cone pushes forward, it squishes the air in front of it. When it pulls back, the air stretches out. Those squish-stretch patterns ripple outward like waves on a pond — except invisible — until they reach an ear.

**Diaphragm (cone)**: The big paper or plastic part of a speaker that you can see. It's shaped like a cone or a shallow bowl and its whole job is to push air. When it moves fast, we hear high sounds; when it moves slowly, we hear low rumbly sounds.

**Magnet**: A piece of metal that pulls on iron and steel without touching them. Every speaker has a strong magnet hidden behind the cone. The magnet's pulling and pushing on a nearby coil of wire is what makes the cone move.

**Electromagnet**: A coil of wire that becomes a magnet only when electricity flows through it. Inside a speaker, the music signal is electricity that keeps changing — so the electromagnet keeps flipping back and forth, which makes the cone vibrate in time with the music.

**Frequency**: How many times something vibrates each second. Fast vibrations (many per second) sound high, like a whistle. Slow vibrations sound low, like a drum. Speakers can change frequency thousands of times per second.

## Activity

### Activity Overview

Children will feel and see sound vibrations using a real speaker. They'll touch the cone while music plays (so they feel the vibrations through their fingers), watch grains of rice or salt jump on the speaker surface, and finally build a simple paper-cup telephone to experience how vibrations travel through a string. The activity makes the invisible — moving air — visible and touchable.

**Activity Type**: Sensory exploration / Hands-on physics demonstration / Discovery play

**Duration**: 20-30 minutes

**Setup time**: 5-10 minutes

### Materials Needed

**Essential**:
- A small portable Bluetooth speaker or any speaker with an exposed cone (an old bookshelf speaker is ideal)
- A phone or tablet with music or a tone generator app
- A handful of dry rice, salt, or sprinkles
- A plastic wrap or thin paper plate (to lay over the speaker)
- 2 paper or plastic cups
- A 6-10 foot length of string or yarn
- A toothpick or paperclip (to thread the string)

**Optional**:
- A second speaker (broken/old) that can be opened to reveal the magnet and voice coil
- A small refrigerator magnet to demonstrate magnetism
- A tuning fork (very dramatic for vibration demonstration)
- A balloon (to feel low frequencies)

**Cost**: Free if you have a speaker; $5-15 for a cheap Bluetooth speaker if you don't

### Step-by-Step Instructions

**Preparation**:
1. Charge the Bluetooth speaker and queue up music with strong bass (drums, marching band, dance music)
2. If using a tone generator, find a frequency around 60-100 Hz (low, slow vibrations are most visible)
3. Pre-thread the string through holes in the cup bottoms, knotting each end inside the cup so it doesn't slip
4. Place all materials on a low table

**Activity Flow**:

1. **Introduction** (2-3 min):
   - Hold up the speaker: "Music is coming out of this, but how? There's no mouth in there. There's no person inside singing."
   - Play music quietly and ask the child to put their fingers gently on the cone (or grill if no exposed cone)
   - Say: "Feel that? That wiggling is called *vibration*. The speaker is shaking back and forth so fast you can barely see it — but you can feel it with your fingers. That wiggling pushes the air, and the moving air is what your ears hear as music."

2. **The Rice Dance** (5-7 min):
   - Lay a piece of plastic wrap or a thin paper plate flat on top of the speaker (cone facing up if possible)
   - Sprinkle a small handful of rice or salt on top
   - Play a song with strong bass at moderate volume
   - Watch the rice jump and dance: "Look! The rice is jumping. The speaker is pushing the air, and the air is pushing the rice. Each *push* is a *sound wave*."
   - Change the song or use a tone generator to switch between low and high notes: "When the sound is low and rumbly, the rice jumps slowly. When it's high, the rice jumps tiny and fast. That's called *frequency* — how many times per second the speaker wiggles."

3. **The Secret Inside** (5-7 min):
   - Show the child a small magnet and a paperclip: "Magnets pull on metal without touching it. Watch."
   - Demonstrate the magnet pulling the paperclip
   - Say: "There's a magnet hidden behind every speaker. And right next to the magnet is a tiny coil of wire — like a spring made of wire. When electricity from your phone goes into the wire, the wire becomes a *magnet* too! That's called an *electromagnet*."
   - Use hand gestures: one hand is the permanent magnet (steady), the other is the electromagnet (flips push-pull). Show how they push and pull each other.
   - "The music is electricity that keeps changing — so the electromagnet keeps flipping push, pull, push, pull, thousands of times every second. That flipping moves the cone, and the cone wiggles the air, and the wiggly air reaches your ears as music."
   - If you have an old broken speaker, carefully show the magnet on the back and the voice coil

4. **Cup Telephone** (5-7 min):
   - Hand each end of the cup telephone to your child and yourself
   - Pull the string tight (this is critical — slack string carries no vibrations)
   - Whisper into your cup: "Your cup is your speaker's cone. When I talk, my voice makes my cup vibrate. The vibrations travel down the string, and your cup vibrates the air near your ear."
   - Switch roles. Let the child whisper into their cup
   - Experiment: pinch the string (sound stops!), let it slack (sound stops!), try different lengths

5. **Wrap-Up** (2-3 min):
   - Recap: "Sound is just moving air. Speakers make sound by wiggling a cone. The cone wiggles because a magnet pushes and pulls a coil. The coil knows when to push because the music is electricity flowing through it."
   - Ask: "What was your favorite part? The dancing rice? The whisper string?"
   - Listen together to a final song: "Now when you hear music, you know there's a tiny magnet inside, pushing air to your ears."

### Age Adaptations

**2-3 years**:
- Focus on the sensory experience: feeling the vibration, watching the rice dance
- Use the word "wiggle" alongside "vibrate" — both are accurate
- Skip the electromagnet explanation; just say "there's a magnet inside that pushes the cone"
- Use a balloon held against the speaker — low bass makes the balloon thump against their hand dramatically
- Keep the session shorter (10-15 min total)

**3-4+ years**:
- Introduce the words *frequency*, *amplitude*, *voice coil*, *diaphragm*
- Use a tone generator app to demonstrate the relationship between frequency number and pitch ("This says 100 — slow and low. This says 1000 — fast and high.")
- Try the cup telephone with different string materials (yarn, fishing line, dental floss) and discuss which works best
- Discuss how headphones are just tiny speakers
- Try sketching a speaker cross-section: magnet, coil, cone, air, ear

### Extension Ideas

**Variation 1: Make a Real Speaker**
Use a strong neodymium magnet, a paper cup, a coil of enameled wire (50-100 turns), and an audio source with an amplifier output. The cup is the diaphragm; the coil is taped to its bottom; the magnet sits inside the coil. With enough current, music will actually come out — quietly. (Adult build; child observes and tests.)

**Variation 2: Visible Sound**
Stretch plastic wrap over a metal bowl, sprinkle salt on top, and place a speaker next to the bowl. Loud sounds will make the salt jump and form patterns (this is a simple Chladni-style demonstration of sound waves shaking a surface).

**Variation 3: Tuning Fork Pool**
Strike a tuning fork and dip it into a bowl of water. The vibrating prongs splash water everywhere — visible proof that vibration moves the medium around it (air in a speaker, water in the bowl).

**Repeat Play**: Try different songs and have the child predict which will make the biggest jumps (low bass = biggest jumps). Try whispering vs. shouting into the cup phone. Visit a guitar/piano store and feel the body of an instrument vibrate when played.

## 📝 Parent/Caregiver Notes

### Common Challenges & Solutions

**Challenge**: My child wants to touch and poke the speaker cone

**Solution**: Speaker cones are delicate — paper cones can tear with a fingernail. Demonstrate touching gently with one flat finger, and supervise closely. If your speaker is precious, use a cheap Bluetooth speaker or one that's been retired. The plastic wrap on top of the cone protects it from rice and curious fingers alike.

**Challenge**: The rice doesn't jump much

**Solution**: Increase the volume, use a song with stronger bass (try drum-heavy music or a 60-100 Hz sine wave from a tone generator app), and make sure the plastic wrap or plate lies flat against the cone. Smaller speakers struggle with low frequencies — a larger speaker or subwoofer dramatically improves the effect.

**Challenge**: The cup telephone doesn't work

**Solution**: The string MUST be taut — even a tiny bit of slack breaks the signal. Don't let the string touch your hands, clothes, or anything else along its length. Use a thin, strong string (fishing line works great) rather than thick yarn. The cups should be paper or thin plastic, not rigid.

**Challenge**: My child doesn't seem interested

**Solution**: Lead with the most dramatic demo (the rice dance with loud bass) rather than the explanation. Many children connect physically before conceptually — let them feel the vibration first, ask questions second. Try playing their favorite song so the demo doubles as a dance party.

### Follow-Up Activities

1. **Quick reinforcement**: At bedtime, hold a stuffed animal against a small speaker playing quiet music. "Can you feel the wiggle? That's the vibration."

2. **Daily connection**: When the child hears music in the car or at the store, point at the speakers: "There's a magnet inside that one too, pushing air to your ears right now."

3. **Vocal vibration**: Hold the child's hand to your throat while you hum. "I have a speaker inside my throat too! It's called my voice. Feel it vibrating?" (This is true — vocal cords are vibrating membranes, the same physics.)

4. **Listen for the bass**: Practice noticing the difference between low rumbly sounds (slow vibrations) and high tinkly sounds (fast vibrations). Sort household sounds by frequency: refrigerator hum, microwave beep, dog bark, bird chirp.
